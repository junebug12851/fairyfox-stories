## Designed — *The Blindfold Act*

A 1926 carnival murder mystery. **Madame Sonora** (born Ada Prewitt) is the show's mind-reader — a
cold-reader and fraud who has spent fifteen years seeing through strangers and letting no one see her.
When **Colonel Aurelio**, the owner-patriarch, is found dead in his locked pay-wagon with the week's
take gone, the county sheriff wants it hung on the show and the show wants it buried by Friday. Sonora
reads her own found-family to keep the law off them — and reads the truth in the one face she never
wanted to: **Lark**, sixteen, her apprentice, the girl she taught every trick she'd need to get free.
The vise is the complicity beat from `craft/character.md`: the comfortable-monstrous option (let the law
hang an outsider, keep the girl and the show and the blindfold) costs her *nothing*, and the arc is that
she refuses it and pays everything. Want (the show, the act, the wall) vs. need (to be seen). Earned,
self-contained ending — which is exactly why the sequel-lock fits: a story meant to end here.

**Distinctness:** fills two gaps at once — the shelf's **first mystery**, and its **first grounded,
non-magical realm** (everything else is speculative). Fresh author + style, deliberately unlike the
recent books. Restraint held to the Carmen-Sandiego bar: the death is accidental, off-page, un-lurid;
Lark is a minor and her fear/past are handled non-sexually and non-graphically (the Colonel's threat is
to *send her back*, nothing predatory).

## Written — one whole chapter each

- **The Wintering House — ch.5 "The Living Hand."** The cracks begin in earnest (blueprint ch.5–7).
  Agnes has said yes to the picture and *knows* — so she starts, quietly, to look. Charlotte begins a
  Christian name for the last companion — "Ellen—" — and swallows it back, because Christian names are
  for the living and the living in that house are four and always four. Then the writing-slope Agnes
  has dusted around forty times and never opened: inside, a sealed letter *To my son Aldous, to be
  given him on his wedding morning*, in the dead Mrs Ravenscar's strong upright hand — and the ink is
  **black, not brown**, lately dried, with a wet-laid tail where the pen lifted meaning to write more.
  The mistress is not merely tended; she is *kept up* — someone in the house sits at the slope and is
  her, lovingly, so faithfully the ink hasn't had time to age. And the fire Agnes did not lay, burning
  red in the night before she rises; Mrs Hale, frightened, not cruel: *"Nobody lays that fire in the
  night, Miss Thorne… it's best you get used to it being laid."* (Kept ambiguous/human per the
  blueprint's "no vengeful ghost" — the night-fire resolves later as denied grief, not a spectre.)
  ~1,870 words.
- **The Girl Who Sold the Wind — ch.4 "The Third Bargain."** The fable's turn. Rafiq points Zari back
  to Mother Sufra, who names the third bargain's currency: a wind takes only **wind-metal** — a coin
  minted from what you lost, or from what you want most and would never put on the shelf. Zari offers
  the stall, the coin, her whole trade — and it runs through her fingers like wet salt, worthless,
  *because she'd give it gladly* and wind-metal must be minted from the thing you would not spend. Under
  the stall, under the money, is the one thing: **never to be the hungry orphan again — "enough," the
  wall she built her whole cheating soul around.** She mints it, breaks off *one corner* (a
  down-payment — a coin of your own can be paid in pieces, each costing the whole of itself), and buys a
  fast wind west. It doesn't feel like freedom; it feels like a door left open with the cold coming in.
  Mother Sufra's hook, set for the finale: *at the gate she will be asked for the whole of it, and no
  one can spend it for her — "be poor. It is the only coin she will take."* Ends on Halvashir's white
  stone and the grey stranger on the wind ahead of them. ~1,820 words.
- **The Cartographer of Decks — ch.5 "What the Founders Left."** Sela finally uses Vosse's key and goes
  down to Deck Nought — 140.04 metres of corridor the charts call solid hull, measured true without a
  tremor. In the observation blister, *made to be found and not hidden*, the First Cartographer's own
  recording, plated **for whoever measures true**. She is old in it — she lived the whole way into the
  lie and made this at the end, knowing. The mercy, plainly: *a people does not live on where it is
  going; it lives on believing it is going somewhere.* Take away the destination and you don't get a
  brave people, you get a generation that stops cutting the number into the doorframe. So the founders
  painted a horizon on the inside of the hull — and did the one redeeming thing: they refused to make
  the choice binding. They left the true map lying in the cold with the key free for whoever measures
  true, *because whether to tell is the one line I could not draw for you.* Sela decides **nothing** on
  the day she learns its size ("a thing decided in the hour you first understand its size is not a
  decision, it is a flinch") — but she sees, at last, what her trade always was: someone draws Deck
  Nought as solid hull, by hand, every generation, on purpose, knowing. And it's about to be her. Sets
  up ch.6, the finale (the map that is *for* someone; the mentor's role passing to her). ~1,785 words.
