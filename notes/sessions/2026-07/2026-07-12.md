## What was written

- **The Wintering House — ch.3 "The Keeping of the Room"** (~2,000 words). Charlotte hands Agnes the
  keeping of the warm room and gives its rules as ordinary household instruction — the fire never
  out, the water changed though unused, the book never closed. Agnes crosses the threshold, is warmed
  before she has even looked, and then watches Charlotte say *good morning, Mama* to a turned-down
  bed — and folds the cold clear part of herself away, because Charlotte isn't mad or cruel, she is
  *easy*, and there is more love in that one foolish sentence than Agnes has had in ten years. Two
  deniable wrongnesses she talks herself out of: hair that comes out of the brush like thread, and a
  page of sermons sun-browned in one clean slanting shape by lying open under the same window for far
  longer than an invalid's rest. Ends with Mrs Hale — *"they all take to it quick, miss"* — and Agnes
  saying good morning to the bed each morning after that, first because it was easier, then because
  it felt like company. Blueprint band 2–4 ("The Comfortable Lie") held exactly.
- **The Cartographer of Decks — ch.3 "The Office Knows"** (~2,400 words). Sela files the true number,
  honestly, and gets back a duty chit. **Master Cartographer Ilkka Vosse** — the man whose worn chair
  and clean track she found in the dust of Deck Nought — has been going down there once a month for
  forty-one years. He doesn't defend himself; he opens the resurvey drawer and lets her read it: 31
  filings saying *161 m, confirmed*, and beneath them an unlabelled folder holding 11 more, across
  four centuries, each logging 169 and each appended with a drawing of a hatch. The one before
  Vosse's is in her **grandmother's** hand. His case isn't "people can't handle it" — it's a
  cartographer's case: *a map is a choice about what to draw, made by a person, for a person.* He
  hands her the key and a month. The chapter's last beat is the real horror: the key is warm from his
  pocket, and what she feels under everything is **chosen**. Sets up ch.6's "a map is *for* someone."
- **The Cinderwick Job — ch.3 "The Cage"** (~2,500 words). Three days of casing that turn up the one
  thing that frightens Juno — the Cage has no lazy man in it. The viewing: nine porters in a rotation
  designed by someone who has actually robbed a building, one door in and out, and lot forty-one
  under its own lamp. The bird is wound for the trade and the gallery is delighted; Juno watches the
  *room* instead, and finds **Magister Crane** in his doorway not delighted but *listening*, braced,
  like a man waiting to hear his own name. Crane greets her, dismantles her cover kindly, admits he
  wrote the card and that Pell's principal is a fiction, and presses bidder's chit **214** into her
  hand: come in the front, in the light, on my ledger. She leaves knowing the job was a hire, not a
  trap — and carrying the one thing he didn't give her: **Crane is afraid of the Nightingale.** Feeds
  the ch.5 reveal (the bird sings back a sound that proves the job was his design) without spending
  it.
