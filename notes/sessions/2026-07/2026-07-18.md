## Written — one whole chapter each

- **Every Lock but Hers — ch.5 "What It Costs"** (~2,435 words; blueprint ch.5). Nell deliberately learns
  the hollow-hour mechanism the only way she knows how — as a tradeswoman surveying an unfamiliar lock, cold
  and unromantic, and the chapter is careful to say so ("there's a version of me that's easier to love… and
  it isn't the true one"). She runs the test on a *small* truth chosen so it can't cost the house — and the
  telling stroke is that the small truth isn't shameful, it's **good**: as children she was the one who let
  Theo in, taught herself the whole trade with a hairpin on their own front door, and has kept that bolted
  because if she says it aloud the girl who let him in has to stand next to the woman who didn't. She spends
  it to a practice lock at the bench, and the chapter's real work is the **findings**: (1) the door gives
  you the *reaching*, never the *arriving* — the memory opens onto nine-year-old Nell picking the latch and
  **shuts before Theo's face**, because the face is behind the big unpaid truth; (2) the cost is the truth
  itself, gone loose and unpocketable; (3) once said, you *mean* it — a "nice fact about a hairpin" turns
  back into the door it always was, *I was the one who let him in… and then, once, I didn't.* She's measured
  the fear now, which makes the big bolt both cheaper to say and far more tempting — and the tempting scares
  her worse than the cost. Ends on her own thrown bolt heard, for the first time in three years, not as
  safety but as a sentence she won't speak. **On voice:** held Del Marsh's Nell exactly — direct address,
  locksmith metaphor, "on the record," the good-is-worse move. (Note: Del Marsh also authors *The Cinderwick
  Job*, which is why Juno's cellar prose rhymes with Nell's — same pseudo-author, deliberate.)
- **The Wintering House — ch.7 "The Hand at the Fire"** (~2,525 words; blueprint ch.5–7, "The Cracks", last
  of the section). Agnes, cold-clear since the churchyard, reasons that you can't take hold of a *large*
  horror (it has no handle) but you *can* take hold of a fire, because a fire is laid by a person's two
  hands — so she pulls that thread: who lays the always-warm fire in the mistress's room? A night vigil.
  **No ghost** — the chapter's whole design is that the answer is human and loving and worse for it: it's
  **Charlotte**, at two in the morning, with a coal-scuttle she carried up herself, tending the fire and
  talking to the empty chair (the workbasket, the needle twelve years in the cloth) in the present tense she
  can't afford by daylight. Two of the section's three cracks land in one patient scene — the fire no
  servant will admit to (it's the family, in secret rota) and **a name spoken and snatched back**: Charlotte
  says *"Ada was always cold —"*, stops as at a missing stair, swears to the empty room she won't say it.
  Then Agnes finds the predecessors in the box-room: a workbox marked **E.M.**, and under the lining a
  letter begun and never sent — *"there is one thing I cannot make out about the house that I shall tell you
  when"* — trailing off mid-word, years old, ink somehow not four days dry. Pays off ch.6's Fellgate line
  ("there've been three or four of you… we don't see them come down") without resolving the ch.8–9 secret:
  the horror is love that won't let a thing be over, and a warm place a house will always, patiently, find a
  new girl to fill. **On voice:** held Cordelia Fenn's Agnes — long looping period sentences, the great
  *was*-that-means-*is* tense named again, dread over gore.
- **The Cinderwick Job — ch.6 "The Double-Cross"** (~2,300 words; blueprint ch.6). Resolves the betrayals
  and corners her, without spending the finale. **The need-arc turns first:** cornered in the sprung cellar
  with the Nightingale over her heart, Juno does the thing she never does and tells Kit **the fourth thing**
  (the held-back truth from ch.4's pact) — her real name **Junia Wren**, the ledger page that reads
  *delivered*, the exact word that hangs her. **Kit's true loyalty:** bought by Crane, then not — offered
  the one clean exit of his life, he laughs instead of taking it. **The client / Crane's want, plain:** Pell
  returns to lay it out — Crane never wanted the bird, he wants *her*, grateful and free and his; the open
  cage is the score + a house on Alder Street + the burned page, and the price is a whistle she answers for
  life and letting the "surplus" boy walk up alone. **The real double-cross, named to her face by Kit:**
  Crane bought him not to sell her but so that *she'd* sell *him* at the last — because her rule is cut the
  second person rather than carry his fear, and Crane has measured that and bet his house on it. She ends
  cornered with no clever move left, the bird still in her coat, poised for ch.7 "The Name": Crane accounted
  for everything she'd *do* and never for the one thing she's never done (trust a person over a plan). **On
  voice:** held Del Marsh's Juno — "on the record", counts exits, load-on-the-pick metaphor, the coldly
  polite villain kept offstage (Crane "home to his supper"; his menace by proxy through the moth Pell).
