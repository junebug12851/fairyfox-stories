### The book

**The One-O'Clock Bus** — magical realism novella. Abel Mensah, 58, has driven the Route 9 night
service for eleven years and kept two records of it: the official log (which lies beautifully) and
a blue notebook (which doesn't) — because between one and two, Bus 17 goes where her passengers
*need*. The authority flags eleven years of impossible minutes and sends **Vera Brandt**, an
auditor who believes a fudged log is always somebody's cover — right about records, wrong about
this route, and paid fairly for both. The engine: the audit as antagonist-clock vs the hour; the
arc: the timetable as Abel's armour against Corvus Road, his daughter's street, a letter two years
unanswered — the bus's one undelivered passenger is its driver. Codex seed grown nearly verbatim
("a night bus driver who knows where everyone needs to go but not where he does"). Full
chapter-by-chapter plan public in the blueprint; low point ch.7–8 (the override, the missed
goodbye, the blue book surrendered); finale a front door, not a trick.

### The chapter

**Ch.6 "The Job She Can't Do" (~2,600 words)** — the moral hinge, straight to the blueprint: the
Marsh Lane closer whose tenant is Dani and her kid, bolting the world out exactly the way Nell does.
Craft decisions worth recording:

- **Dani is never seen and never named on the page** — a voice behind a thrown bolt, a chained buggy,
  red frog wellies, and the felt-tip drawing taped **facing out** (three stick people smiling,
  *outside* the house). The mirror does its work without a confrontation scene; Nell's rule of
  no-questions collapses under things merely *seen*.
- **The refusal refuses the technicality too.** The trade's "occupied" rule is the clean, free way
  out — and the chapter's turn is Nell declining it: "It's not that she's in. I'm not doing this
  one." The flaw pays on the last beat (serialization's paid-turn rule): *she doesn't refuse* was a
  load-bearing wall, and she drills it herself. Honesty kept: refusing ≠ saving — another firm will
  do the job inside a week; Dani's clock runs on toward ch.11.
- **The magic is braided in without firing:** the hollow hour live during the survey, true things
  "lining up behind my teeth like tenants," the keys-on-the-dash habit born, the call placed at ten
  past two. Ch.5's canon (the cold spot, the felt hour, the big truth grown cheaper) load-bears.
- **Childhood rhyme cashed:** Nell kneels ear-to-wood at Dani's door — the first shape she ever made,
  from ch.5's hairpin memory — now on the *outside* of a bolt no one but the thrower can draw.
- **The pull into ch.7:** Mar breaks her three-year cut-off deliberately — "There is no—", stopped
  "the way you stop when you've decided to do a thing properly or not at all," and summons Nell in
  **daylight**. Ch.7 "What Mar Holds" can open on the reveal with no stalling.
- **Quiet ending beat:** first night in three years Nell leaves her own bolt undrawn. Book-notes flag:
  don't quietly re-bolt her without meaning it.
