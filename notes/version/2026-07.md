- **Every Lock but Hers — ch.5 "What It Costs." (4/11 → 5/11.)** Nell stops slamming the hollow-hour door
  and *surveys* it, cold and professional, the way she'd survey a lock she doesn't understand. She runs
  the experiment on a small truth so it can't cost her the house — and the truth she picks isn't shameful,

  the bench, and learns what the magic actually *takes*: the door gives you the reaching, never the
  arriving (the memory opens onto young Nell picking the latch — and shuts before Theo's face, because the
  face is behind the *big* truth she hasn't paid). And the cost lands in the body: a spent truth goes loose

  bolt she's never drawn both cheaper to say and far more tempting. Ends with her bolting her own door and,

- **The Blindfold Act — ch.2 "A Cold Reading."** The book's thesis in trade terms: a *hot* reading is a
  purchase; a **cold** one is saying the true thing before you've decided whether you want it to be true.
  Sonora is the best alive at it, and this is the chapter where she won't. The sheriff walks Bruno to the
  fender at nine in the morning in front of everyone, and writes down nothing, because he isn't gathering

- **The Girl Who Sold the Wind — ch.5 "The Homesick Wind." (2/5 → 5/5, complete.)** Zari refuses the
  clever plan she carried a thousand miles, because it wins by making a widow's love into a leash. Then
  the *honest* trade fails: she mints the whole never-again coin, offers it for the bottle, and it goes
  soft as wet salt — because the homesick wind is worth ten stalls, and there's no losing in a trade that
  hands you back *enough* with change. The first honest question of her life gets the real answer: the
  wind won't lift the stranger, because home was an evening and you cannot be carried to a Tuesday — so
  it was never revenge, it was company. Zari pays with the one offer that has no return in it: *"Come home
  with me… there's room."* And the fable turns on a word it planted in chapter one: *a wind, once sold,
  cannot be **recalled*** — *called back*. The city struck her name off the calendar and kept it in its
  own mouth twice a day for forty years — **Halva-shir** — and never once meant her by it. So a girl with
  nothing says **"Shir,"** and means it, and the homesick wind carries her home: three feet. Home is not a
  place; it's whoever is calling you and means it. She got the stall back, years later, and it did not
  matter — enough was never a number.
- **The Cartographer of Decks — ch.6 "The Chart She Draws." (5/6 → 6/6, complete.)** She takes Vosse down
  on day nineteen; he'd been once, at forty-five, for eleven minutes, and carried the key forty-one years,
  *"and those two facts are the same fact."* Nobody had ever asked him what it was like. The chair on Deck
  Nought is worn smooth — four hundred years, no maintenance, one chair, arms polished by hundreds of
  hours of hands: **eleven people, each certain they were the only one.** The sixth filing in the
  unlabelled folder is her grandmother's, who lived forty-one more years and never said a word. Eleven
  flinches, laid end to end across four centuries, look exactly like a policy — and that, not the coasting,
  is what she can't accept. So she files the true number and signs it, and draws the grey block herself, by
  hand, identical to the one before it and a completely different object; and instead of broadcasting to
  eleven thousand in an afternoon, she charts **the room, not the hull** — the chair, and twelve names, two
  marked uncertain with reasoning — and leaves it under the founders' own plate. They left an argument; an
  argument is one more way of being alone. She left a map. The grey block is the kind story; the eight
  metres in the corridor is the door. Deck 14-aft resurveys in 433, when Emil Tolver is thirty-one.
