# Book-notes — Every Lock but Hers (internal)

Private continuity + voice bible. Not published (under `notes/`, excluded from the build).
Written 2026-07-11 during the ch.2 grow — the book was planted 2026-07-10 without one, which is a
gap the grow loop shouldn't repeat (a planted book gets its bible on planting day).

## Author-persona & style

- **Author-persona:** Del Marsh — the wry street-poet (`_authors/del-marsh.md`). Second book under
  this byline, and Marsh's **second universe** (the Gaslight Reach → the Hollow Hours).
- **Prose style:** hard-boiled/propulsive, but **warmed** for the Hollow Hours — the trade-craft
  aphorism, the deflecting joke, the soft spot arriving sideways and late. Distinguish from *The
  Cinderwick Job*: Juno Vale is **quick**; Nell Corrigan is **slow and load-bearing** — longer
  breaths, the philosophy of a craft, the sentence that goes one clause too far because she's
  avoiding something.

## Voice (hold across all 11 chapters)

- First person, **past tense**, Nell Corrigan. Talks about locks to avoid talking about herself;
  every piece of trade-lore is secretly about her (a lock is a question; the bolt is on the inside).
- Deflects with the ledger, the fee, the professional detail. **Never** self-pitying; the ache
  arrives through what she declines to say.
- Warm, not bleak (Hollow Hours discipline). No gore, no grimness — heartbreak drawn sideways.

## Continuity facts (locked)

- **The rule of the magic:** in the **hollow hour (1–2 a.m.)**, if Nell has a lock in her hands and
  says aloud a **true thing she has never said aloud**, the door opens onto the place she most needs
  to reach. One truth, one door; the truth is **spent** — once said, it's loose in the world and
  can't be taken back. Nothing else is charged: no blood, no danger. Only honesty.
- **Ch.1 (2026-07-10):** lock-out job, 4th floor off the canal, ~2:10 a.m. She says *"that's all it
  ever takes, somebody to sit down and listen"* → the door opens onto **her old kitchen** (bad
  hopeful yellow, radio with brass in it, burn mark by the hob, **two mugs still steaming**). Mar
  rings at ~2:30 with a **closer**.
- **Ch.2 (2026-07-11):** the closer on **Dolvin Road** (first-floor flat over a chip shop; five-lever
  mortice out, better lock in; the tenant a night-shift man, **steel-toed boots left outside** — she
  leaves him his boots; she watches him come up the road in the mirror and does not stop). Fees are
  locked: **£60** a lock-out, **£240** a closer — *the same price she'd charge to open the same
  door.* Then **Ferrier Street lock-up** (roller door, bench, key-cutting machine, kettle, a chair
  she found, a cupboard with a cheap lock and a coat she never wears). Just past 1 a.m., cutting the
  landlord's spares, she says *"I hate the closers"* → the cupboard opens onto the **landing outside
  her own old green door, number 11**, third floor, brown carpet, moth in the strip light, filler
  under the spyhole — **it is raining**, a shape stands at the edge of the light, and there is **one
  knock**. She slams it. She works out the rule, refuses it, and stays clear of the hollow hour for
  **eleven days** (this gap covers the run-up to ch.3).
- Nell's own door: a **proper** lock, fitted by her, on her own place. She has a **shoebox of job
  tickets** going back years and has never been able to throw it out.
- **Mar** — dispatcher, gravel voice, a heater she won't let anyone fix. Their hello: *"You're up."
  / "You're always up."* Their goodbye: *"You keep the ledger." / "I keep the ledger even."* Mar's
  line, always cut off: *there is no ledger.*
- **Theo Corrigan** — the brother. Three years ago he came to her door owing the wrong people and
  she **turned the bolt**; she has told the version where he *left* ever since. The rain and the one
  knock (ch.2) are that night's furniture — do not over-spend them before ch.4/ch.9.
- **Sully (Mr. Ilesanmi)** — ch.3 onward. **Dani** — ch.6. Both already in the blueprint.

## Arc reminders

- Want: the ledger closed, nothing owed either way. Need: to be let in — to say the bolted thing out
  loud and let a door be opened *for* her.
- The magic is never the fix; it only ever hands her the truth and waits. Ch.9 is where she pays the
  whole one; everything before it is her finding smaller change and refusing to break the note.

## Added in ch.3–4 — locked

- **Ch.3 "Sully's Keys":** Sully = **Mr. Ilesanmi**, 78, ex-signalman (Thorne Junction, 1974 snow, mate
  **Peters**), 2nd floor Harrowgate Court, balcony garden, disastrous tomatoes. Hides his own keys from
  himself (fridge, clock, socks, biscuit-tin lid…) so *someone comes* — grief, not going soft; keys
  found in the pocket of a **navy coat that isn't his**, scarf still tucked in. His line: *a key gets
  lost (no blame); a key you hide is a thing you've done, and the gift of hiding it from yourself is you
  never have to say why.* Nell finally accepts his tea (11 min) — hardest work of her week. No hollow
  hour spent; she stands at her own door with the true thing behind her teeth and pays nothing.
- **Ch.4 "The Bolt":** the wound named, in Nell's voice (not the magic). **Theo** = younger brother, the
  warm one, "born without a door." Three years ago he came owing the wrong people, knocked **once** (then
  a frightened second) in the rain at the old green door, no.11, and Nell **threw the bolt** — chose the
  lock over letting the people he owed through it. She's re-cut the lie *he left* ever since; it's come
  **literally true** — she genuinely can't find him now. Hollow-hour test in the parked van (van key
  counts): she turns the ignition like anybody, pays nothing, does **not** say the true thing. Full
  telling is saved for **ch.9**; do not overspend. Trade-lore this chapter = the over-lifted lever binds
  as hard as the unlifted one.

## Added in ch.5–6 — locked

- **Ch.5 "What It Costs" (2026-07-18):** Nell runs the mechanism as a deliberate experiment ("the
  survey," at the Ferrier Street lock-up, an **uncut brass blank** in hand — the magic cares about the
  hand, not the metal). The small truth spent: *"I let him in. When we were small. Every time. I was the
  one who let him in"* — she taught herself the whole trade letting Theo back in through their own front
  door (hairpin, bent spoke, ear to the wood; his line through the door: *"Nell. Nell. I can hear you
  doing it."*). The door opened onto the old hallway — and **shut before Theo's face**. The finding:
  **the magic gives the reaching, not the arriving**; the arriving is held hostage against the whole
  truth. Cost in the body: a spent truth leaves a **cold spot** where a "warm stone" was; and spending
  the small one made the big one **cheaper to say** — she *wants* to pay it now, which frightens her
  more than the cost. Since the survey she can **feel the hollow hour arrive** (the give at the edges).
- **Ch.6 "The Job She Can't Do" (2026-07-21):** the Marsh Lane closer (end terrace, ground floor, black
  door; sheet said empty, lamp said otherwise). **Dani** never opens the door and is never named to
  Nell — a voice behind thrown bolts ("You're no one. No offence."), the kid's *"Is it the man?"*, the
  **chained buggy**, **red frog wellies**, and the felt-tip **drawing taped facing out**: three stick
  people holding hands, smiling, *outside* the house. Nell kneels ear-to-wood (the childhood shape) and
  walks. **First refusal in fifteen years** — and she deliberately does NOT tick the "occupied" box
  (the clean out): *"It's not that she's in. I'm not doing this one."* — *"Who do I say refused?" /
  "Corrigan. The one who doesn't refuse."* She knows refusing ≠ saving: another firm will do it inside
  a week (ch.11 must square the eviction for real). New trade-lore: **a lock is a question; a bolt is a
  door's mind made up** — only the hand that threw it can draw it. Keys-on-the-dash habit born (she no
  longer trusts her mouth in the hollow hour; she waits till ten past two to phone). **Mar breaks the
  ritual:** "Come in and see me. Tomorrow. Daylight… There's a thing I've been not saying to you for
  three years" — starts *"There is no—"* and stops **deliberately** (doing it properly, not the usual
  cut-off). And Nell, home: **first night in three years she leaves her own bolt undrawn** — locked
  like anybody, not bolted. She sleeps.
