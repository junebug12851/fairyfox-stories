title: The Hand at the Fire
---

I came back up the ridge that Christmas evening and I changed the water in the glass, and I laid the
fourth place, and I called the woman upstairs *she*, exactly as I had promised the churchyard I would;
and I did it, God forgive me, well. But something had come up in the gallery that could not now be got
back to sleep, and it had a question in it, and the question — small, and household, and therefore the
worst kind — was this. Who lit the fire?

I want to explain why that was the thread I pulled, out of the whole tangled skein, because it will
sound like a strange one. There were larger horrors. There was a name in the ground and a name that
lived nowhere in the house; there was ink that would not brown; there was a village that pitied me and
would not say why. But I had learned, in six weeks of being kept, that you cannot take hold of a large
horror. It has no handle. You reach for it and your hand closes on the warm nothing they have taught
you to close it on. Whereas a fire is a thing a person lays, with a person's two hands, out of coal
that a person has carried up a stair — and if I could find the hands, I thought, I could follow the
hands back, past the warm nothing, to whatever true and dreadful thing they served.

For the fire in the mistress's room was always in.

I had never once seen it out. Not banked, not grey, not the cold hearth of a room shut up — but *in*,
red, tended, the coals broken and fed, at every hour I ever came to the door, and I came at all hours,
for the water. And in six weeks I had never seen a soul lay it. I had asked Susan, once, early, in the
innocent way one asks such things, who did the mistress's fire; and Susan, who is a good girl and
frightened, had gone still with a duster in her hand and said that the mistress's room was *not the
girls' to do*, and had found somewhere else to be. I had not asked again. One learns, in that house,
the exact shape of the questions that empty a room.

So I did not ask. I watched.

---

I chose the night after Boxing Day, when the house was tired and full and had let its guard down as
much as it ever did, and I did the one disobedient thing I had done since I came: I did not go to my
bed at eleven. I put out my candle, so that the line of light under my door should die at the proper
hour, and I sat up in the dark in my chair in my coat, with the grey shawl over my knees, and I
listened to Wintering House go to sleep around me — the settling of old timber, the wind working at
the moor-side casements, the long clock on the half-landing telling the hours down into the cold — and
I waited to see who came to the warm room.

Midnight. One. The clock was very loud when everything else had stopped. I had begun to think I should
learn nothing but the ache in my own back, and I had begun, too, to be afraid in the plain bodily way
one is afraid alone in the dark in a house one has understood too much of — when, a little after two, I
heard a door.

Not my corridor. Below, and along — the family's side. A door drawn open with the great care of a
person who knows exactly which of its hinges will speak. Then feet on the stair, unhurried, familiar
with the dark, not carrying a candle: feet that did not need to see. I rose, and I opened my own door
by the trick I had by then learned of lifting it on its hinge so the latch would not sound, and I went
out onto the gallery in my stocking feet and stood back in the deep shadow by the linen press, where a
person may see the door of the mistress's room and not be seen.

And I watched Charlotte Ravenscar go in to her mother.

---

She had a scuttle. That was the first mercy and the first horror of it together — that there was no
ghost, no drifting cold thing, only a young woman of six-and-twenty in her nightgown and a wrapper,
with a coal-scuttle in her hand that she had carried up herself from some low store, her bright social
daytime face put off with her stays, and something older and plainer on underneath it. She went in
without knocking. Of course she did not knock; you do not knock at a room you have decided is not
empty and know to be empty; the not-knocking was the whole of the lie, performed for no audience, at
two in the morning, which is when we perform our truest lies, the ones we cannot afford a witness to.

The door she left ajar. And I, who had spent six weeks becoming the sort of creature that watches
through the gaps in doors, went softly down the gallery in the dark and stood against the wall by the
hinge, close enough to the warmth breathing out of that room to feel it on my cold cheek, and I looked
in through the crack, and I listened, and I am not proud of it, and I would do it again.

She knelt at the hearth. She broke the coals and fed them, small and neat, an accustomed hand, a hand
that had done this a hundred nights; and the fire took her face and made it kind and made it terrible,
and she talked while she did it, low, easy, the running unweighted talk of a daughter to a mother in
the last room of the day — the small stuff of a life, the rector's visit, the frost, the goose that
had been dry, a length of ribbon she had seen in Fellgate and thought *you'd* have liked — *you'd*,
she said, and I marked it, the tense had shifted, here in the dark and the warmth with no one by she
did not say *was*, she said *you*, present, plain, to the empty chair by the fire where a workbasket
sat with a piece of work in it, half done, the needle still in the cloth.

Twelve years the needle had been in that cloth. I knew it now. And every night a hand came and kept
the room warm around it so that the thread should never quite be cold, and it was not a servant's
hand, it had never been a servant's hand, it was the hand of the child of the house, come in the one
hour she could not be brave in, to be, for a little while, a girl whose mother was only in the next
chair and not in the ground three miles off under the cut words *she is not here*.

I could have wept for her. I nearly did. And then she said the other name, and I did not.

---

She was banking the fire to leave it, and she said, still in that low even voice, still to the chair —
"There. That'll see the night. Ada was always cold, she felt it worse than —"

And stopped.

Stopped the way you stop when your foot finds the stair that is not there in the dark. Her hand went
still on the poker. Her head came up, not toward me — she had not heard me — but toward the room
itself, toward the chair, as though the chair had caught her at something; and into the warm silence,
after the name, she said, quickly, low, in a voice gone suddenly younger and afraid, "— no. No. I
won't. I said I wouldn't say it and I shan't," and she put the poker down with a small clean click and
knelt there a moment with her face in her two hands, quite silent, and then rose, and smoothed her
wrapper, and put her day face back on in the firelight the way another woman puts on a bonnet, and
turned for the door.

I was gone before it opened — back along the wall, into the shadow of the press, my heart going so
that I thought the house must hear it — and I watched her come out and draw the door to its exact
accustomed inch of ajar, and go down the speaking stair on her sure blind feet, and heard the careful
door below close on its known good hinge, and then there was nothing but the clock and the wind and
the red line under the mistress's door, kept warm for the night by a living hand.

*Ada.*

I stood in the cold gallery a long time with that name.

For it was not the mistress's name. The mistress was Honoria; I had that from the ground, cut deep and
kept clear of moss. *Ada* was some other. *Ada was always cold* — was; not the great *was* of that
house that means *is*, but a small plain grieving *was*, the ordinary tense of the actually gone — and
*she felt it worse than* — than whom? Than the mistress? Than Charlotte? And why, having begun to grieve
one woman aloud, had she flinched from a second name as from a hot bar, and sworn to the empty room
that she would not say it, as though the saying of *that* name were a worse trespass than all the long
warm nightly lie of the first?

*There've been three or four of you, love,* the old woman at Fellgate had said, pressing my hand the
graveside way. *Good girls. They come up that hill in the autumn with their boxes. We don't see them
come down.*

I did not sleep at all that night, and toward morning, when the black window had begun to go the iron
colour that passes for dawn on the moor in the dead of the year, I got up and did a thing I had told
myself for six weeks I would not do, because it was not my place and I had a place to keep. I lit my
own stub of candle, and I went along to the little box-room at the cold end of my own corridor, where
the house put the things it had no present use for and no heart to burn — and I looked, at last, for
the girls before me.

---

I found them in the second trunk.

A workbox, that was not mine and was not the mistress's — a plain deal one, a servant's or a poor
gentlewoman's, with a girl's initials done in brass tacks on the lid, *E.M.*, and inside it thread and
a thimble and a folded collar and, pressed flat under the lining where a girl hides the one thing that
is hers, a letter begun and never sent, in a young round hopeful hand — *Dearest Mother, You are not
to worry for me. They are very kind, and I am to be quite one of the family, only there is one thing I
cannot make out about the house that I shall tell you when* — and there it stopped, mid-word, the ink
of the last letter trailed off long the way ink does when the hand that held the pen was called away
and did not come back to it; and the paper was not four days dry, and it was not new either, it was
years old, it had lain under that lining through I did not like to think how many winters, keeping
warm the last unfinished true thing a girl in this house had tried to say to somebody outside it,
before the house folded her, too, so small and put her so far back that she went up the ridge in the
autumn with her box and did not come down, and left behind her a workbox and a collar and half a
sentence, and a first name I would never have, and only two brass initials, *E.M.*, that were not
*Ada* either, so that there was not one girl gone before me into that warmth but two at the least, and
perhaps three, and perhaps four, each of them come up the hill hungry to be *quite one of the family*,
each of them kept, and warmed, and wanted, and each of them stopped, at last, in the middle of the one
true sentence she had tried to send out past the walls.

I closed the workbox. I put it back exactly as it had lain, under the exact fold of the exact shirt,
the way I have learned to leave a thing that has been searched looking as though it never was; and I
went back to my room in the iron light with the whole night's finding cold and heavy in me, and I sat
in my chair with the grey shawl — the dead woman's shawl, and mine now, and whose before mine? — and I
waited for Susan's knock and the ordinary murder of the morning.

I understood, now, more than I had gone looking for. I had wanted the hand at the fire, and I had it,
and it was a loving hand, and that was the cruelty I had not braced for: there was no monster in
Wintering House. There was only love that would not let a thing be over — a family that had learned to
keep a room warm the way you keep a dying coal, feeding it small and nightly and never once letting it
go grey, because grey is admitting the fire is out; and into that warmth, one at a time, they had drawn
girls like me who had no fire of their own and could not tell the difference, at first, between being
loved and being kept.

I had come up that hill in the autumn with my box. I had thought myself so clever and so cold.

And I understood, sitting there in the iron light, why the pity at Fellgate had been the graveside
kind. It was not the dead they pitied. Honoria was past pity; the dead are safe. They pressed the hand
of the living girl with the good shawl and the frightened face because they had watched the autumns
come, and the boxes go up the hill, and they knew that the warm room at the top of it had never in
twelve years kept only *one* fire — and that a place, once a house has learned to keep it warm, is a
thing it will always, patiently, find somebody to fill.
