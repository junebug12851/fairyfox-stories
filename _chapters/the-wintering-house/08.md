I have read, in improving books, that knowledge is a lamp. It is not. I carried what I knew back
into that house the way you carry a coal in a closed hand, and it lit nothing, and it warmed
nothing, and it burned only me; and in the week between the old year and the new I rose at seven and
broke the ice on my ewer and fed the mistress's fire, a little coal at a time, and changed the water
in the glass, and said good morning to the bed, and was told at breakfast that I was very good — and
did it all knowing, as I had done it all before in ignorance, and I am not able to report that the
work went any differently. That is the fact about Wintering House that I have found no listener
willing to take from me, and so I set it down for the record instead: the liturgy does not require
your belief. It only requires your hands.

But I had become a woman with a day face. I understood, now, what Charlotte put on with her stays
each morning, because I put on my own; and Mrs Hale, who had watched me down a gallery in the dark
for six weeks, watched me now across the ordinary daylight in a new way, the way a nurse reads a
fever coming before there is any flush to read. She said nothing. She set my porridge down with her
kind hands, and her cold face was sorry in advance, as it had been sorry on the doorstep the evening
I came; and I ate what was put in front of me, as I had done from the first, and we neither of us
asked the other what she knew.

---

Mr Marchbank came up to us in the first week of January, on a morning of hard bright frost.

He came as the coal came and as I had come — to the foot of the drive and no further by wheel, an
old man in an old black coat walking the last black mile with his breath going before him — and the
house put on its best for him. Fires were lit that I had never seen lit, in the hall and the small
parlour, so that for one day in the year the cold house wore a general warmth the way a plain woman
wears borrowed lace; and Charlotte was in her good silk by ten, and Aldous stood at the window from
half past, watching the drive; and it was all, I saw now, exactly the trouble a family takes when
the doctor comes to a house where somebody is very ill, and hope is spoken of downstairs in a
particular bright voice, and nobody says the other word anywhere at all.

He took tea with us, and was dry and kind, and asked me two questions about myself, which was two
more than anyone at Fellgate had risked; and at half past three, with the light already going, Mrs
Hale opened the parlour door and said, "She's ready, sir," — and the household rose, all together,
like a congregation.

I want to set the reading down carefully. It was the only ceremony I ever saw in that house, and it
had a ceremony's exactness, and I have come to believe that the exactness was the point: that a rite
done perfectly, year upon year, is how a household persuades itself that what it does is worship and
not what it is.

We went up the gallery in procession, Mrs Hale first with a branch of candles, then the rector, then
the family, then me. Two chairs had been carried in from the gallery and set inside the mistress's
door — no further; the room's own furniture was not moved, was never moved — and Aldous and
Charlotte sat upon them, and Mrs Hale and Susan stood behind, and I stood behind Susan, at the
threshold, half in the warm and half in the cold, which I have since thought was the truest place in
the house for me to have been put. The fire was high; I had built it high myself. The chair by the
hearth stood at its exact angle with the workbasket beside it, the needle still in the cloth. The
glass shone full on the walnut table. And on the table, under the window, in the last of the hard
white light, the book lay open at the eleventh sermon, with the spectacles folded upon it.

Mr Marchbank went to the table, and stooped, and lifted the spectacles away with the care of a man
lifting a dressing from a wound, and laid them by. And then, with his hands behind his back,
leaning a little, like a man reading a notice at a church door, he read the eleventh sermon aloud to
the room, from the page at which it lay open, and he did not once touch the book.

It was a sermon against immoderate grief. I had dusted around it for six weeks and never once let
myself read past its heading; standing at the threshold I heard the whole of it. It said what such
sermons say — that sorrow which will not be comforted sets itself against the mercy of God; that to
keep what He has taken is to accuse Him; that the dead are not lost to us but gone before — and he
read it levelly and without haste, in a voice made for stone churches, to a turned-down bed and an
empty chair; and the browned shape on the page lay under his eyes the whole time, the shape of
twelve years of the same light falling on the same words, and I stood in the doorway and understood
that he read this sermon because it was the sermon *she had been reading* — that the page had lain
open at it since the morning the book was laid down, and the book was not to be closed, and she
would want to go on from where she was; and that this man had accordingly come up the ridge once a
year for twelve years to read the one sermon in the world this household could not hear, to the one
member of it who could not hear anything, while the family sat with their hands in their laps and
their faces perfectly attentive, letting the words go over them the way the dead let the rain.

Aldous kept his eyes closed throughout, as he did when he was setting something down or picking
something up. Charlotte watched the rector with her bright face open like a girl at a play, and her
lips moved here and there, and I saw that she had it by heart. And when it was done, Mr Marchbank
folded the sermon's corner smooth with one finger — not turning it; smoothing it — and set the
spectacles back upon the page in the exact place his hands had found them, and said, to the room, in
his dry level reading voice, "A happy New Year to you, ma'am," and the family rose, and Charlotte
went to the fire and gave it a touch it did not need, and we processed out again; and behind us Mrs
Hale drew the door to its accustomed inch, and the red line lay along the foot of it, patient as
ever, and the ceremony was over for another year.

---

I was sent to see him down to the gate. I have wondered since whether that was Aldous's courtesy or
Aldous's design, and have decided it does not matter; in that house the two had long ago married.

We walked the black mile in the last of the light, the frost ringing under our feet, and for a
while he said nothing at all, and neither did I. Then, at the turn where the drive first lets you
see the moor whole, he stopped to breathe, and looked out over the white miles at the far grey
smudge of Fellgate tower, and said, without any change in his dry level voice:

"You have been to the churchyard, I think, Miss Thorne."

"On Christmas morning, sir."

"Yes," he said. "The south wall. Out of the wind." He was quiet a moment. "I have read to that room
twelve times. You will want to know how I square it with my cloth, and I will tell you the whole of
my defence, which will not take long, for there is none. When they stopped coming down to the
church, the church had its choice: go up to them, or let them go. A shepherd goes where the flock
is. I have gone up every year since, and every year, coming down again, I ask myself whether I have
been visiting the sick" — he took up his stick — "or sitting down to table with the sickness. And I
have never yet reached the bottom of this hill with an answer."

"They are kind, sir," I said. "That is the thing I cannot make anyone understand. It is all
kindness."

"It is," he said. "I buried a great many people in my time, Miss Thorne, and I will tell you what I
know that they do not: grief is a river. Dam it, and it does not dry; it floods the fields you live
from." He looked at me sideways, an old man's quick look under white brows. "They have built their
dam very high and very well, and they feed it every soul that comes up this hill. You would do well
to think about that in the light of the morning, and not in the warmth of the evening. The warmth of
the evening is on their side."

We walked on. At the gate, with his hand on the cold iron, I asked him the thing I had been carrying
the whole mile down.

"Someone keeps the letters on her stone clear of the moss, sir. Is it you?"

"It is not," said Mr Marchbank. He settled his hat. "It is done in the dark hours, between a
Saturday and a Sunday, a few times in the year. I have never seen the hand. But the path from
Wintering comes down by the beck, Miss Thorne, and a hard frost keeps a footprint far better than I
keep a secret." And he wished me a good New Year, and pressed my hand — once, hard; the graveside
press; they all of them had it, that country — and went away down the ringing road in the dark, and
left me at the gate of Wintering House with the one fact I had wanted and had not dared to hope for:
that somebody in that warm house of the unburied went down, in secret, in the cold, to where she
actually was.

---

It was that night, or the night after — I will be honest where my memory is not exact — that I asked
Mrs Hale about Ada.

I did not surprise her. I had given up thinking I could surprise her; she had been following me down
dark galleries since my first week, and I had come to understand that this was not spying but a kind
of grim chaperonage, the care a woman takes of a girl walking where she herself once walked. I found
her in the kitchen, banking the range for the night, the last lawful fire in the house dying its
lawful nightly death under her rake; and I stood in the door until she knew I was there, which was
at once, and I said, quietly, "Who was Ada, Mrs Hale?"

The rake stopped.

She did not ask where I had the name. I have thought about that a great deal: she did not ask,
because there was only one room in the house where that name could still be breathed, and only one
person who would have breathed it there, and Mrs Hale had known about the two-o'clock fires longer
than anybody, I think, longer perhaps than Charlotte had known she was watched. She stood with her
back to me and the rake still in the grey heart of the range, and when she spoke her voice was the
one she had used on the stairs by the picture — the voice for true things, that she kept in a small
locked place and spent a sentence at a time.

"Ada was mine," she said.

I did not move. Behind me the house stood dark up its whole cold spine, and one floor up the one
fire burned that never died, and I stood in the kitchen doorway and heard the whole of it, told in
the flat parish voice in which the poor tell the worst things, because the worst things are the ones
you cannot afford to dress.

She had come to Wintering at fourteen, kitchenmaid, in old Mr Ravenscar's time. Married the
coachman; widowed early; kept her place, which was all the world she had. Ada was born in the
laundry-loft the winter of the great snow — the mistress herself came up with candles, she said, and
sat the night, and I heard in her voice, even then, even in that doorway, the old warmth of it, the
one grand memory polished by fifty years of service: *the mistress herself*. Ada grew up in the
house, under the tables, in and out of the sculleries. She was quick, and she was liked, and she was
always cold. Some children are; there was never any warming her; she felt a draught through a shut
door, and chilblains from October, and the mistress — the mistress who walked ridges laughing in
green — used to say that Ada Hale had been born with the moor in her bones and ought by rights to
have been born in August.

Then the mistress died. And the first winter after — the winter the fire was first kept, the winter
the fourth place was first laid — Miss Charlotte could not tend the warm room alone at night, and
would have no stranger yet, and Ada was that pleased to be taken upstairs. Nineteen, and proud of
the keeping, and warm at last, for the one room in the house with a living fire was hers to mind.
She minded it two years. And in the third winter a chest fever came off the moor and went through
the low rooms of the house, as such things do, and took the boot-boy lightly, and Susan's
predecessor lightly, and settled on Ada, who had been born cold, and was not strong, and slept the
far side of the house from the one hearth that never went out.

"I asked," said Mrs Hale. That was where the flatness failed her — one word, and she took the rake
out of the range, and set it in its place by the fender, exact, the way every last thing in that
house was set in its place, and stood with her hands empty. "I asked for her to be moved into the
warm. Eleven nights I asked. It was never once said no, miss. I'd have you understand that. Nobody
in that house ever said no to me, nor ever has. It was only never done. There was always the
morrow, and the doctor sent for, and a screen to be found first, and her ladyship's room to be
thought of — it was *hers*, do you see, it was kept, there was no part of it that could be given
away to a sick girl without — " and there she stopped, not as Charlotte stopped, not catching a name
back off her tongue, but simply reaching the place where the road of saying ends. "She died the far
side of the house," she said, flat again. "In the cold. In the warmest house on this moor, that
never let a fire go out in twelve years. My girl died of the cold with a warm room standing ready
not thirty feet off, because the warm room was wanted for somebody who could not feel it. And I laid
her out myself, and she's at Fellgate, by the north wall, where the wind is; the south side's for
the family. You'll have walked past her, miss, coming out the porch. Most do."

The range ticked as it cooled. I said — because it was that or say nothing forever — "And the house?
What did the house do?"

"The house was kind," said Mrs Hale. "The house is always kind. Miss Charlotte cried a fortnight —
she'd grown up with my Ada, do you see, they were girls in this house together." She looked at me
then, full, for the first time since the rake stopped. "And in the spring I found her up in the
laundry-loft, where my girl was born, laying a little fire in the grate there of an evening. And
talking, low. The way she does. *You'd have liked it*, and *you always felt it worse than* — " Mrs
Hale shut her eyes, and opened them. "And I put it out, miss. I raked it out with my own two hands
and I stood over that grate while she cried at me — the only cruelty I ever did in fifty years in
this house, and the kindest thing that's been done in it since the mistress died. And I took her
promise, there in the loft. That she'd not lay that fire again. And that my Ada's name should never
be said in this house — not because it hurts me, though it does, but because I have seen what this
family does with the names of them it cannot give up, and I would not have my girl made a *she*. I'd
sooner she were cold and mine, miss, than warm and theirs. Them below stairs may say what they like
of me. It is the one thing I have ever kept out of this house's keeping, and I mean to take it out
of the world with me clean."

She took up her candle. At the door of her own room, with her hand on the latch, she said the last
of it, not turning round, in the low true voice, and I have carried it with me out of that house and
across all the years since:

"You asked me once who lays the fire in the night, and I told you it was best you got used to it
being laid. That was the kindness they've taught me and I gave it you secondhand, and I'm sorry for
it. Here's the truth the way I'd give it my own girl. There's no ghost in this house, miss. There's
only a family that will keep what it loves warm at any cost in the world — so long as the cost is
carried up the hill by somebody else. I bought my warm death here, and I paid what I paid, and I'm
too old now to want my money back. You've paid nothing yet, bar a shawl and a few good mornings to a
bed." The latch went up, gentle, with the sound a latch makes under a hand that has closed it
softly for fifty years. "Keep your money, Miss Thorne."

---

I went up the cold gallery to my cold room, past the warm door with its patient line of red, and I
did not sleep, and for once in that house it was not dread that kept me. It was arithmetic. It was
the sums I had been doing since Christmas coming out, at last, to their total.

They had never wanted a companion. A companion was what the advertisement asked for, but an
advertisement is a house's day face; I had seen the space on the canvas, sized and primed, the exact
width of a woman standing. What Wintering House wanted was what it had always wanted, winter on
winter, girl on girl: someone to carry the cost up the hill. And I saw — this was the thing that
kept my eyes open on the dark, this was the total at the foot of the page — that they had chosen me,
as they had surely chosen the others, not although I was poor and placeless and had nobody in the
world, but *because* of it. E.M., under the lining of her workbox, had begun *Dearest Mother*; E.M.
had somewhere to send a sentence, and even so her sentence had not got out. I had no letter in me
that anyone was waiting for. No one would write to ask why I had stopped writing. The parish had
pitied me on sight, and now I had the sum of the pity: I was a woman the world would not miss, come
up the hill with her box, to a family that keeps what it loves — and I had wanted so long, so
dearly, to be loved by somebody, that I had been six weeks learning the difference between that and
this, and was not certain I had learned it yet.

The frost held all that week, hard and white and ringing. On the Saturday night I did not go to my
bed. I sat up in the dark in my coat, with the grey shawl over my knees, at my window that gave on
the moor and the beck path; and I watched for the hand that keeps the letters clear.
