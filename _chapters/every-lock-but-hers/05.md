title: What It Costs
---

I'm a tradeswoman, and I want you to keep that in mind through what comes next, because the thing I
did I did the way I do everything: not brave, not soft, not because a night of magic had opened my
heart. I did it the way I'd approach a lock I didn't understand. I did it to find out how it worked.

You laugh. Go on. But there's a version of me that's easier to love — the one who breaks down at the
churchyard, or hears the old man in his hallway and goes home changed — and it isn't the true one.
The true one lay awake three afternoons after Sully's tea running the mechanism in her head the way
she'd run a stubborn cylinder, and thought: *I have found a lock in this city that opens on a truth.
I have been standing in front of it slamming it shut. That is not caution. That is a tradeswoman with
her tools in the van, refusing to survey the job.*

So I surveyed it. I'm not proud of the coldness of it and I'm not going to pretend to a warmth I
didn't have. I decided to learn the rule the way you learn any rule — on a small one first, where it
can't cost you the house.

---

Here is what I already knew, from the cupboard and the van and my own landing three times running.

I knew it wanted the hollow hour, the leak between one and two, and nothing outside it. I knew it
wanted a key in my hand — any key, a van key counted, a house key counted, which meant it wasn't
fussy about the lock, it was fussy about *me*. And I knew it wanted a true thing I had never said out
loud, and that the moment I said one the door in front of me would stop being an honest door and open
onto the place I most needed to reach.

What I didn't know — what a person only finds out by doing, the way you only find out a spring is
past its life by feeling it die under your fingers — was what it took *out* of you. Everyone talks
about magic like it's a gift you accept. Nobody tells you it's a trade, and I of all people should
have known that a trade has two sides, because I'd have my licence off me if I fitted a lock and
forgot the frame.

So I picked a small truth. I want to be honest about how I chose it, because the choosing was the
whole experiment. I went down my list of unsaid things the way you go down a ring of keys in the
dark, thumbing each one, feeling for the one that was true and mine and had never been out of my
mouth — and *not* the big one. Not the bolt. Not the rain. I wasn't going to spend the house to learn
the mechanism. I wanted a truth small enough to lose.

I found one on the third pass, and it surprised me, because it wasn't a shameful one. That's the
thing about the drawer of unsaid things: you assume it's all the bad, and it isn't. Half of what a
person like me keeps bolted is the *good*, because the good is worse. The good is what you're not
allowed to have said, after.

The truth I picked was this: that when we were small, and Theo locked himself out — which he did,
constantly, because he was born without a door, our mum said, and he'd pull ours shut behind him with
the latch down and his key on the hook inside — I was the one who let him in. Not with a key. I
didn't have a key; I was nine. I let him in with a hairpin and a bent bit of bicycle spoke and a
patience nobody knew I had, kneeling on the mat with my ear to the wood, and I never once told him it
was hard, because the look on his face when the door gave — the specific look, relief with nothing
guarded in it, the look of a person who never really believed he'd be shut out and was right — was
the best thing I owned. I taught myself the whole trade on our own front door letting my brother back
in. And I have never said that to a living soul, because if I said it aloud then the woman who bolted
the door on him in the rain would have to stand next to the girl who spent her childhood learning how
not to, and I have kept those two apart for three years with everything I have.

That one. Small enough to lose, I told myself. It's only a nice thing about a hairpin.

I was wrong about it being small. But that's the finding, and I'm getting ahead.

---

I did it at the lock-up.

Not on a call — I didn't want a stranger's door in it, a stranger's disaster, somebody crying on me
while I ran a private test. I went down to the workshop at half twelve with the roller door pulled to
and the one bulb on, and I sat at the bench with the practice locks I keep for teaching myself new
makes, and I waited for the hour the way you wait for a tide.

It came. You feel it, by the way, once you know it's there — the hollow hour. It isn't a bell. It's a
kind of give in the room, the pressure off a thing, the way a house sounds different when the boiler
cuts out and you didn't know you'd been hearing it. At one exactly the workshop went soft at the
edges and the bulb steadied and I picked up a key.

Any key. I picked a blank, actually — an uncut brass blank, a key that opens nothing, that isn't even
a key yet, just the idea of one — because I was testing the rule and I wanted to know if the magic
cared about the metal or about the hand, and it turns out it doesn't give a damn about the metal. It
was in my hand and my hand was mine and that was enough.

I held the blank in front of the practice mortice, which is bolted to a board and opens onto nothing,
onto the back wall of my own lock-up, onto a coil of flex and a calendar from a screw supplier, and I
said the true thing out loud, to the lock, in the empty workshop, the first time it had ever been out
of my body.

I said, "I let him in. When we were small. Every time. I was the one who let him in."

---

I'll tell you what it's like, because I promised you the whole survey and this is the part that isn't
in any manual.

It doesn't feel like magic. It feels like *losing* something. The second the words were out — before
the door did anything, before the light changed — I felt the truth go, the way you feel a tooth come
out: the give, and then the wrong empty smoothness where the thing had been. It had been *mine*. It
had been a thing I carried, a warm sharp private stone I'd had in my pocket for thirty years, taking
it out in the dark to look at it and putting it back, and the instant I said it aloud it wasn't in
the pocket any more. It was loose. It was in the room, in the world, said, unsayable-back, and the
place in me where it had lived was open to the air and cold.

*Once per truth,* I'd worked out, in my clever head, at the bench, on the theory. Now I knew what
*once* meant in the body. It doesn't mean you can only do the trick once. It means the truth is only
yours once, and after you've spent it you don't get the having of it back — only the fact of it,
flat, public, spent, a receipt where a secret was.

And then the door opened.

---

The practice mortice, bolted to a board, opening onto a coil of flex — it swung, on hinges it doesn't
have, and it opened onto our old hallway.

Not a picture of it. Not a dream. The hallway, the actual one, the narrow one with the meter cupboard
and the coats and the brown light through the glass over the door, and the smell of it — that's the
part that undid me, you can't fake a smell, the wet-wool-and-toast smell of a Tuesday thirty years
gone — and on the mat, kneeling, was a girl of nine with a hairpin in her teeth and her ear to the
wood, and on the other side of the door, muffled, patient, certain, a boy of five was saying *Nell.
Nell. I can hear you doing it. I can hear you.*

And the girl on the mat wasn't frightened, and wasn't bolted, and wasn't careful. She had her whole
face open to a door she was about to open. She was the best of me and she didn't know she was the
best of anything, she just knew her brother was on the wrong side of a latch and she had a hairpin
and all the time in the world.

I stood in my lock-up at one in the morning and looked through a door that shouldn't exist at the
person I was before I learned what doors were for, and she got the latch — I heard it, the little
give, the *there you are*, the sound I have chased down every lock in this city for thirty years
without once knowing what I was chasing — and the door in the memory opened, and the light fell out
onto the mat, and I did not see Theo's face.

The door in my lock-up shut. Gently. On its hinges it doesn't have. Back to the coil of flex and the
screw-supplier's calendar and the one bulb and the cold spot in me where a warm stone used to be.

That's the rule, I found out. It gives you the reaching. It doesn't give you the arriving. It opened
me the door and it let me stand at it, and it closed before the face — because the face is on the
other side of the *big* one, the one I hadn't paid, and you don't get the arriving on the cheap. You
get it once, whole, for the whole truth, or you don't get it at all.

---

I sat back down on the stool.

I want to tell you the experiment was a success, because it was: I had my answer, I understood the
mechanism now the way I understand a five-lever, I could have written it on the board. Hollow hour,
plus a key, plus one true thing never said, equals the door — and the price is the truth itself, gone
loose and spent and unpocketable, and the door gives you the reach and keeps the arrival hostage
against the one you're afraid to say.

Clean survey. Full findings. And I sat on my stool in the soft hour with my hands flat on the cold
bench and I found that I was not the woman who'd sat down.

Because here's the thing about spending even a small true thing, the thing nobody tells you, the
thing that is going to matter very much before this is over: once it's out, you *mean* it. I'd told
myself it was a nice fact about a hairpin. It wasn't. The second it was loose in the world it stopped
being a fact and turned back into what it had always secretly been, which was a door of its own — *I
was the one who let him in* — and now that I'd said it I couldn't stand next to it and not feel the
whole weight of the sentence that comes after, the one I hadn't paid, the one waiting behind my teeth
with the rest of the rent.

*I was the one who let him in. Every time. And then, once, I didn't.*

I'd spent a warm stone to learn how the lock worked, and the learning had cut a groove straight
toward the door I'd built three years not to look at, and I sat in the lock-up and understood, with
the flat professional part of me that has never once lied to me about a mechanism, that I had just
made the big one *cheaper to say* — not because I was braver, but because I'd felt, now, in the body,
exactly what it costs, and a fear you've measured is a different animal from a fear you haven't.

It's more tempting. That's the part I hate. I thought knowing the cost would frighten me off. Instead
I sat there at ten past one with the cold spot aching and I wanted, God help me, to do it again — to
spend the big one, right now, tonight, and see the face — and the wanting frightened me far more than
the cost ever had.

I put the blank back in the drawer with the others that open nothing.

Then I drove home, and I bolted the door, top and bottom, the way I always do.

But I'll tell you what was different, and it was the first thing that had been different in three
years. I stood in my own hall with my hand on the thrown bolt, and for the first time I did not feel
it as *safe*. I felt it as a truth I hadn't said yet. I'd learned the shape of the mechanism, and now
I couldn't un-know that every lock in the flat was a sentence I was refusing to speak, and that the
kettle and the chair and the shoebox on the shelf were all on the reaching side of a door, and the
arriving was being kept from me for one reason only, which was that I would not pay.

I could open it with a bad mood.

I still didn't. But I lay awake, and for the first time it wasn't the bolt I was listening to.

It was the price. And how much, now, I almost wanted to pay it.
