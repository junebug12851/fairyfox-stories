I've been putting this off, and you'll have noticed, because you're not a fool or you'd have stopped
reading around the second lock. I've told you about the trade and the hollow hour and a man who hides
his keys from himself, and I've walked you all round the edge of the one thing the way I walk round a
door I don't want to open, tapping the frame, admiring the hinges, telling you what the wood costs.

So. Theo.

Theo is my brother. Was. Is. That's the whole of the grammar problem, right there, and it's why I
don't say his name if I can help it, because I get to the verb and there's nothing safe to put in it.

---

Here's a thing about a mortice lock that nobody wants when I tell them.

A good mortice is a stack of levers, and every one of them has to lift to exactly the right height —
not too little, not too much — before the bolt will throw. Lift a lever too far and it binds just as
hard as not lifting it at all. That's the part people don't credit. They think the danger is the lock
staying shut. The danger is just as much the *over*-lift, the trying-too-hard, the key cut a hair
proud so it jams the works and you stand there sawing at your own front door thinking you've been
robbed when all you've done is ask too much of the mechanism.

I cut a key too proud once. Three years ago. I lifted every lever in my whole life a hair too far, all
at once, and the bolt bound, and it has been bound ever since.

That's the closest I can get to it sideways. Let me try it straight, and you'll see why I don't.

---

Theo was four years younger and he was the warm one. I know how that sounds from a woman who fits
British Standard five-levers to her own bathroom, but there it is: he was the one who left doors open.
Left the flat door open, left the fridge open, left himself open, walked into rooms full of strangers
and came out with friends and a phone number and somebody's dog. Our mum used to say I was born with
the bolt already thrown and Theo was born without a door at all, and she meant it fond, and it turned
out to be the most accurate thing anyone ever said about either of us, and it killed her a bit that it
was true.

He got in with people. That's all I'll dress it up as, because he's not here to have it dressed up
worse, and because the particular people and the particular money aren't the point and never were. He
owed. He owed the sort of money that stops being money and turns into a countdown, and the sort of
people who don't send letters. And he did what he'd done his whole life, which was the thing I'd never
once done: he came to a door and knocked, because it never occurred to him, not once, in twenty-six
years, that a door might not open.

It was raining. You know that part; I let it slip, at the lock-up, when the cupboard came open onto
the landing at number eleven and it was raining and there was a shape at the edge of the light. I told
you I slammed it and worked out the rule. I didn't tell you what the shape was. The shape was my
brother, on the third-floor landing of the flat we grew up being able to afford and then couldn't,
outside the green door with the filler under the spyhole, in the rain he'd walked three miles through
because his phone was gone and the buses had stopped, and he knocked. Once. He always knocked once and
waited, Theo, he had manners even at the end, he'd stand back off a door and give you room to not want
him.

And I was on the other side of it. And I knew it was him. And I put the bolt on.

Top and bottom. The way I always do. I stood in my own hallway with my hand flat on my own good door
and I threw the bolt on my brother because on the other side of him were the people he owed, somewhere
out in that rain, and I had done the sum a locksmith does without being asked, the load on the pick,
the give before the give — *if I open this, they come through it too* — and I chose the door. I chose
the lock. I stood there and I listened to him knock the second time, which he never did, which meant
he was frightened, which meant it was worse than I'd let myself think, and I did not draw the bolt,
and after a while — not a short while; I want that on the record, he stood out there a long time in
the wet — after a while the knocking stopped, and the landing light clicked off on its timer, and I
told myself he'd gone to sort it, that he was resourceful, that he always landed on his feet, that
he'd *left*.

He left. That's the word I fitted. It's a good word. It carries no weight at all. He left, the way you
say the weather did something, the way you say a key got lost. Not *I shut him out.* Not *I bolted the
door on him in the rain and I don't know where he went.* He left. And I've cut that key so many times
now it turns sweet as anything, and I've told it to Mar and to myself and to the shoebox of tickets I
can't throw out, and it opens onto three years of a flat with every lock on the inside and nobody ever
coming up the stairs.

I don't know where he is. That's the true middle of it, the lever I lift too far every single time. I
told the version where he left, and the trouble with a lie you tell that well is that it comes true in
the one direction you can't stand: I made it so he'd left, I made a door where a brother had been, and
now there is genuinely no one on the other side of it that I can find, and I did that with my own two
hands in an afternoon, like housekeeping.

---

So. The hollow hour, since that's what you're here for.

It was a lock-out, a real one, a girl in a dressing gown on the wrong side of a slammed door at half
one, nothing magic about her, cat inside and phone inside and the whole ordinary disaster of it, and I
had her in under four minutes and she nearly cried on me and I said what I say, *happens to everyone,*
and I went back down to the van.

And I sat in the van in the dark with my hands on the wheel and I did not start it, because it was
gone one and not yet two, and the true thing was in my mouth. It's always in my mouth now. Since the
cupboard. It sits behind my teeth like a tooth I keep finding with my tongue, and I know exactly what
it is and exactly what it would cost, and I know — the way I know a cylinder before I've got the second
pin — that if I said it out loud, right there, with the van key in my hand, which is a key, which
counts, the door of that van would open onto a night that had my brother in it. Wherever he actually
went. Whatever the rain actually did. The place I most need to reach and have bolted shut for three
years.

One truth. Once. And it's spent, and it's loose, and I can't take it back.

I had it right up against my teeth. I could feel the shape of the first word.

And I'll tell you what I did, because I've been honest with you the whole way down and I'm not going to
turn shy at the bottom of the stairs: I put the key in the ignition and I turned it, like anybody, and
I paid nothing, and I got nothing, and the van coughed and caught and stayed a van, and I drove home
through the wet streets to my flat with the good door, and I bolted it, top and bottom, and I did not
say the thing.

Not because I couldn't. I want that clear. I've spent my whole life being the one who *can*. I didn't
say it because saying it means it's true out loud, in the world, in a place that isn't only my own
head where I can keep re-cutting it sweeter — and because the door might open, and he might not be
behind it, and I have built three years on not having to find that out.

That's the lock I've never picked. It's not that it's hard. God, it's not hard. It's a euro cylinder.
It's an insult. I could open it with a bad mood.

It's just that the keys are on the inside, and I'm the one who put them there, and I have never once,
in three years, been able to make myself go and pick them up.
