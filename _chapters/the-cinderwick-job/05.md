title: The Auction
---

The night auction at the Cage runs from ten till the last lot sells or the last bidder faints,
whichever the Ordinal finds more amusing, and it is the one night a week the whole building is loud.
Three hundred people in the long gallery under the great lamps, all of them rich, all of them lying
about how rich, a hammer going, a clerk crying the lots, porters running, and under the floor of all
that noise a cellar so quiet you could hear your own nerve fail.

That noise was the plan. You do not rob a silent house; a silent house hears you. You rob a house that
is busy shouting at itself. Kit had the timing off the drawings to the minute — the north-wall
passage, nine feet of filled brick behind the consignment window, and behind that the old 1809
strongroom the 1847 mending had swallowed and lied about, and in the strongroom, where it had sat for
forty years listening to everything the Ordinal ever said in the dark, the Cinderwick Nightingale.

The passage was open. Of course it was open. Somebody had been at it that week with a mason's care and
left the last course loose, dry-set, ready to lift out by hand, and Kit lifted it out by hand and
looked at me through the gap with brick-dust on his stupid honest face and said, "After you, Miss
Vale, it's your city," and I went first into a hole a man had opened for us because a man wanted us in
it. I have gone through a great many doors I was afraid of. I have never before gone through one I was
*expected* at, and found it worse.

---

I'll give the job this: it went beautifully.

It went so beautifully I could have wept, and I don't weep, I cost things. Kit was better in the dark
than any partner I've had and I've had few and buried the memory of most; he read the passage by
fingertip, called the drop before I found it, caught my wrist at the sink in the floor I'd have gone
into up to the hip. We came into the strongroom at eleven with the auction roaring faint and holy
above us like weather in another country, and there it was on its iron stand under a bell of glass,
and it was smaller than two years of wanting it had made it.

A brass bird. Sparrow-sized, wings furled, head cocked as if it had heard the door and was too proud
to turn. Under the glass, a key, and a little card in the Ordinal's copperplate: *Lot 41. The
Cinderwick Nightingale. Speaks with any tongue it has been taught.* Which is the auctioneer's way of
saying the thing I had not said out loud to Kit and did not mean to: that it sings back sound. Any
sound. Every sound it has stood in the dark and swallowed for forty years in the one room where the
Ordinal does the business it does not do upstairs.

I lifted the glass. Kit had the stand. We had rehearsed the lift like a pair of pallbearers and it
came off its iron perch into my two hands light as a lie, cold, heavier at the breast than a thing
that size has any right to be, all its little clockwork weight down low where the voice lives.

"Told you," Kit breathed. "In and gone. He'll be furious."

He wouldn't. That was the trouble, and I opened my mouth to say so, and the bird sang.

---

I did not wind it. I want that on the record for whoever comes after me and finds this account: nobody
touched the key. It sang because it was ready to, because it had been *set* to, the way a trap is set,
and it opened its little brass beak in my hands three floors under three hundred witnesses and it sang
back, in a voice I knew, a voice genteel and unhurried and never once raised, the voice of a man
standing in that exact room not many nights before, arranging his furniture:

*"There's a woman in this town who can find anything. Print their numbers on one sheet — consecutive,
mind, she'll want to see it — and put them down at opposite ends of the city. She'll take the trap
because she can see it. That's the whole of her. Sorrel you tell nothing; he'll charm her by Wednesday
and never know he was the charm. Leave the north wall dry-set. Let them do the clever part. I only want
them holding it when the room closes."*

The bird stopped. Cocked its head the other way. And in the silence after Crane's voice — because it
was Crane's voice, Kit had gone the colour of the brick-dust and I did not need to ask — the thing sang
one note more, a small satisfied domestic sound it had also learned in that room, the sound of a green
lamp being turned down and a door being drawn to.

"That's us," Kit said. He wasn't smiling. Without the smile he looked about nineteen and I have never
liked him better. "That's — Vale, it *recorded him hiring us*. It was in the room."

"It's been in the room forty years," I said. "It's in everything he's ever done down here. He knows
that better than we do." My hands had gone very steady, which is what they do when the rest of me is
falling. "He didn't sell us this bird, Kit. He *sat* us in front of the one object in Cinderwick that
could prove he owns us, and he opened the wall himself, and he told it to sing when we picked it up."

"Why?"

"Because a woman who's seen the trap spends all her nerve *not* springing it," I said, "and he's spent
mine down to the wick. We're standing in the sprung trap holding the cheese and being clever about it,
which is exactly, to the letter, what he paid four hundred guineas to watch."

Above us, a long way up, in another country, the hammer fell on some rich fool's lot, and the whole
gallery roared its approval, and did not know we were down here, and would not be told, because being
seen was never the lock on this room. Being *useful* was.

---

Here is the part I have not told Kit, and did not tell him then, and am only setting down because a
liar who keeps one last drawer locked even from the page is no better than the man she's writing
about.

When the bird sang Crane's voice, I heard Crane's voice.

But the Nightingale does not sing one thing at a time, not really; it is forty years deep, and when
you have woken it it *riffles*, the way a struck bell will give you, under the note you rang, the
whole cathedral of its other notes going faint and far. And under Crane — under the green lamp and the
door drawn to — for one half-second, from very far down, from the bottom of the forty years where the
oldest sound is kept, it gave me another voice. Not Crane's. A voice I had spent eleven years and a
new name and the whole architecture of Juno Vale making certain I would never hear again.

*Wren, Junia. She'll not be missed. Deliver her Tuesday.*

Two seconds of a man I had loved, in that room, doing the sum on me that a locksmith does without being
asked, choosing the door, throwing the bolt — and I stood in the sprung trap with the proof of it cold
in my two hands, the actual sound, the actual night, the thing I had told myself for eleven years I
did not want and would not cross the street for, and I found out in half a second and three floors of
brick that I had been lying to myself with the same sweet-cut key I lie to everyone else with, because
my knees nearly went.

That is the whole of why Crane is dangerous, and I understood it there, holding the bird. He did not
build a trap out of a curio and a rival and four hundred guineas. He built it out of me. He knew the
one voice in that box that could stop my heart in my chest at the worst possible instant, and he set
the bird to riffle, and he trusted my own history to nail my feet to the floor of his cellar for the
half-second his people needed.

Which is when I heard the passage behind us, nine feet of it, going dark.

---

Not closing loud. Nothing about Crane is loud. A lamp, up the passage, being turned down. A door being
drawn to. The exact two domestic sounds the bird had sung, only this time they were real and behind
us, between us and Coldharbour Lane and the busy shouting house and the whole rest of my life.

"Vale," Kit said, very quietly, not young now, a professional now, already three moves along and
choosing which face to keep. "The wall."

"I heard it."

"There's one way out of this room and it's the way he opened for us, and he's just—"

"I *heard* it, Kit." I put the bird inside my coat, against the breast, where its cold weight sat over
my own stopped heart like a second, colder one. Two years I'd wanted the score that put me beyond the
Ordinal forever, and I had it buttoned against my ribs, and it turned out the score was the bait and I
was the catch, and the man who'd caught me was so certain of the shape of me that he'd gone up to his
green lamp and turned it down and gone, I did not doubt, home to his supper, leaving the trade to close
the room and the girl to spend her last nerve being clever in the dark.

There is no move in that. You cannot out-clever a man who has already sung you your own confession.
Every road out of that cellar was a road he'd paved, and the only one open was the one with his people
on it.

Except.

Kit Sorrel was standing in the dark beside me, bought and charming and better than he should be,
looking at the closed wall with a picklock already out and turning in his fingers, and he had come
nine feet through a hole for a woman he'd known five days, and he had gone the colour of brick-dust
when the bird gave him the truth, which is not what a man does when he already knew.

I had spent eleven years never once betting my life on a second person's fear.

"Kit," I said. "I'm going to tell you the truth about the fourth thing."
