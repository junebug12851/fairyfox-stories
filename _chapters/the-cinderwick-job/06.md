title: The Double-Cross
---

There are things you say to a partner in the dark of a job that you can never take back, and I had
made a rule against every one of them, and I broke the rule standing in a sprung trap three floors
under the auction with a stolen bird cold against my heart and a man I'd known five days going the
colour of brick-dust beside me.

"The fourth thing," I said. "In the Fetters. I said we'd each hold one back and both know it and work
anyway. You held yours; I don't want it, keep it. Here's mine."

"Vale—"

"Shut up, Kit, the door's closed and I'm only doing this once." I could hear the passage behind us
staying dark, patient, the way Crane is patient, and I made myself slow down, because a thing said
fast is a thing said scared, and I was done being scared in that man's cellar. "The bird sang his
voice. You heard it. Under his voice it gave you another one — *Wren, Junia, she'll not be missed,
deliver her Tuesday* — and you had the grace not to ask me who that was, and I'm telling you before I
have to, because I've spent eleven years never telling anyone and I've watched exactly where that got
me, which is here."

"You don't have to—"

"Junia Wren. That's the name. Eleven years ago a man I loved sold me to the Ordinal for a night-mail
ticket and a sum, and stood in this room to collect it, and this bird was on the shelf and it heard
him do it, and it has his voice in it still, and Crane knows that, and that is the fourth thing, Kit.
That's the drawer I keep locked from the page. There's a piece of paper in this building says *Wren,
Junia, delivered*, and any night Crane wants me hanged he has only to say the name out loud, and he's
had it eleven years, and tonight he sat me down in front of the one object in Cinderwick that could
sing it back to me in the middle of a job to prove he could stop my heart whenever he pleased." I got
it all out and my voice did not go, and I want that on the record. "So now you know the leash. Now
you know exactly which word ends me. And I'm handing it to you, in the dark, with the way out shut,
because I have run out of clever and you're the only lock in this cellar I haven't tried."

Kit Sorrel looked at me for a moment that went on too long.

Then he said, "God, you do it just like he said you would," and I felt the floor tip — and then, "you
make even *trusting* somebody sound like a heist," and I understood he wasn't Crane's, not in the way
that mattered, not any more, and the floor came back.

---

I did not have long to be glad of it.

The lamp up the passage came back on.

Not bright. Crane's people do not do anything bright. It came up soft and yellow nine feet along the
brick, and against it there was a shape I knew, damp and apologetic, in a good coat that fit better
now than it had in the Green Ledger, because a man grows into a coat once he's stopped hoping you'll
turn out to be somebody else.

"Miss Vale," said Pell. "You're not to be alarmed. There are four men in the passage and none of them
is to touch you, on his particular instruction, so you may put away whatever you have in your sleeve;
it would only make the evening long."

I put away what I had in my sleeve. I count things I can't control, and I had counted the four men by
their breathing before he'd finished the sentence.

"Pell," I said. "You've come up in the world. Last time they sent you they picked you because I'd not
listen to you. What's the thinking tonight?"

"Tonight they've sent me," Pell said, "because he thought you'd rather hear it from a coward than a
Magister. He's considerate, my principal. It's the worst thing about him." He came to the mouth of
the passage and stopped, with the lamp behind him and the four men behind the lamp, and he did not
come into the strongroom, and I marked that too: even now, even holding every card, they'd send the
moth to the doorway and keep the room I was in a room they had to be *invited* into. Crane does not
spend what he doesn't have to. "You have the Nightingale."

"I have a bird."

"You have *the* bird, against your heart, where I'd keep it too. And here is the whole of the
evening, Miss Vale, laid out plain, because he says you of all people are owed the plain version." He
folded the soft hands. "You may keep it."

Beside me, Kit went very still.

"You may walk up that passage with the Nightingale in your coat," Pell said, "and out through
Coldharbour Lane, and no hand will be laid on you, tonight or after. The four hundred guineas are in
a house on Alder Street with your new name on the lease. And the page — *Wren, Junia, delivered* — the
page comes to you, as promised, in an hour, in this room, and you may burn it in that very fire."

"For the bird."

"Not for the bird." Pell's wet mouth made the shape of something that on a braver man would have been
a wince. "He doesn't want the bird, Miss Vale. He never wanted the bird. You worked that out three
floors up, I should think; you've been three moves ahead of me since the oysters. He wants the finder
who can walk into the Cage on a night like this and come out with the one thing in it he can't afford
loose in the world — and he wants her *grateful*, and *free*, and *his*. You take the bird, and the
house, and the page, and the freedom. And the next time a curio needs finding that no honest finder
can be told about, you find it, because a woman with a house and a name and a burned page has a great
deal more to lose than a woman with none, and he knows it, and so, now, do you." He spread the hands.
"That's the leash. It was never the paper. The paper was only to teach you the collar's *there*. The
collar is everything he's about to give you."

---

I have been offered a great many things in my life across a great many tables, and I have a rule about
that too, which is that the size of the gift tells you the size of the cage, and this was the largest
gift anybody had ever put in front of me, and I stood in the cellar and felt the bars of it go up all
around, quiet and considerate and true, every one of them a kindness.

Because it was a good offer. That's the part I need you to understand, the part that makes Crane what
he is. It was the offer I'd have written for myself two years ago — the score, the name, the burned
page, the door beyond the Ordinal forever — and he'd taken every single thing I'd ever said I wanted
and built me a cage out of them, and left the door of it open, because he knew that a cage you can
leave any time is the only one you never leave. Hanging me was nothing. Any fool with the page could
hang me. Crane had found the one thing better than a body on a rope, which was a finder in a good coat
who came when he whistled and hated herself in a house on Alder Street and called it freedom.

"And him?" I said. I did not look at Kit. "Sorrel. Where's he in the plain version?"

"Mr Sorrel," said Pell, and here — I'll give the moth this, to the end — here he looked genuinely
sorry, "Mr Sorrel is surplus. He did his part; he found you the wall and he charmed you off it, and my
principal is a thorough man but not a wasteful one. Mr Sorrel may walk up the passage tonight with
nothing, and no hand laid on him, and never come back to the Reach, and that is the best offer of his
life and he should take it before the lamp thinks better of it." Pell's eyes went to Kit, almost
gentle. "You were told you'd enjoy her. You weren't told to be a fool about it. Go home, lad."

There it was. The double-cross laid out on the brass as neat as two bidder's chits on a table: keep
the bird, keep the name, keep the freedom, and the price was a house on Alder Street and a whistle I'd
answer for the rest of my life — and all I had to do to buy it was let the surplus boy walk up the
passage alone, which he could do safe, which was the sensible thing, which even *he* could see was the
sensible thing.

And Kit Sorrel, who was bought and charming and better than he should be, who'd been Crane's before he
was ever mine and had every reason in the lamplight to take the one clean exit he'd ever been offered —

Kit laughed.

The real one. Sudden and awful and glad, the one from the Fetters, the laugh of a young man who's been
holding his breath and has just decided not to bother any more.

"See, that's your mistake," he said, to Pell, to the lamp, to the four men breathing behind it, easy
as a man declining a second gin. "Not his — yours. You said *surplus* like it settles it." He put his
own boot up on the fallen course of brick, casual as the fender at the Fetters, and grinned at the
doorway with his ruin all over his face. "He built a trap out of everything she wants. Very clever.
Two years of measuring. Only he forgot to measure me, on account of I'm surplus, and here's the thing
about a man nobody's measuring." He spread his hands, mocking Pell's own gesture back at him. "He can
do anything he likes."

---

"Kit," I said. "Don't."

"Wouldn't dream of it. I'm just standing here." But he'd shifted, so that his shoulder was an inch in
front of mine, between me and the four men, which is not standing still, which is the oldest true
thing a body says. "Vale. He's offered you the whole world to walk up that passage alone and leave me
in the cellar. I want you to notice he hasn't offered *me* one thing to walk up alone and leave *you*.
You ever wonder why?"

I had wondered why. I'd wondered it the second Pell said *surplus*.

"Because I'm the cheap one," Kit said, cheerful, awful. "I don't know your name and I can't sing it
and once I'm out of the Reach I'm a story nobody believes. But you, holding that bird, trusting a
plan, three moves ahead and alone — that's the whole of you, that's what he bought two years of, and
he is *counting*, right now, on you doing the sums the way you always do them, deciding I'm a risk you
can't carry, choosing the door." He looked sideways at me and the fire had gone out of the grin and
left just the young face under it. "That's the double-cross, Vale. It was never me. He didn't buy me
to sell you. He bought me so that at the last, in the dark, with the way out shut, you'd sell *me* —
because that's what you do, that's your rule, a plan with a second person in it is a plan with a
second person's fear in it, and you'd rather cut the second person than carry the fear. He measured
that. He's standing in his supper right now betting the house on it."

The strongroom was very quiet. Above us, a long way up, in another country, the hammer fell and three
hundred people roared over a thing that didn't matter, and did not know we were down here, and would
not be told.

Pell waited in the doorway, patient, sorry, a moth holding a lamp.

And I stood with the Nightingale cold over my stopped heart and the whole considerate cage built up
around me out of everything I'd ever wanted, and one exit paved with a house and a name, and the boy
who'd laughed instead of leaving standing an inch in front of my shoulder with a picklock he hadn't
bothered to hide, and I understood, the way I understand a lock from the load on the pick, exactly
what move Crane had left me and exactly why it wasn't a clever one.

Every road out of that cellar was a road he'd paved. I'd known that since the Fetters. I'd said it out
loud. *You cannot out-clever a man who has already accounted for you being clever.*

He'd accounted for all of it. The trap, the rival, the bird, the voice, the name, the offer, the
sums.

He'd accounted for everything I'd do.

He had not, could not, would never account for the one thing I had never done in eleven years — and
Kit Sorrel, God help us both, was standing in the sprung trap telling me its name.
