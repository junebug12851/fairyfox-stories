title: The Cage

You don't case a building. Buildings are stupid; they'll stand there all night letting you look at
them. You case the *habits* — the boy who props the yard door with a brick because he's got a girl on
Coldharbour Lane, the lamp-man who does the north wall at ten and the south at ten past because he
walks the same way every night of his life. A building is only ever as locked as the laziest person in
it.

That's the first thing they teach you in the Understair, and it's true of every building in Cinderwick
except one.

I spent three days on the Cage before the viewing. I sold a nothing curio to a fence on Gallow Row who
owes his rent to a man who drinks with a Cage porter, and I bought the porter four gins and let him
tell me he was important. I sat two mornings in the coffee-house opposite with my back to the window
and a shaving-glass propped against the sugar, watching the north wall in the reflection like a
coward, because a woman who watches the Cage with her own eyes gets remembered. I walked Coldharbour
Lane at every hour a person can walk it and I counted.

Here's what three days bought me.

The Cage has eleven doors. Nine of them are for show. The consignment window in the north wall does not
open onto the building at all — it opens onto a brick box with a clerk in it, and the clerk goes home
through a different door, and I know that because I followed him and he ended up in a brick box within
a brick box, which told me more about the Ordinal than a year of gossip. The coal is delivered *inside*
the yard, under a watchman, and burned the same day. There is no chute. I checked. Of course I checked.

The vault is under the auction floor, which is under the long gallery, which means anything you take
out of that vault has to come *up* through the room where three hundred of the richest people in the
Reach are sitting looking at each other in the light.

The lamp-man walks it different every night.

Nobody props the yard door.

That was the part that got under my collar, and I didn't know why for two more days. Every building in
the world has a lazy man in it. The Cage doesn't. Not because they've hired better men — they've hired
the same drunks and dreamers the rest of us have — but because somebody in that building goes round
behind them, every night, and quietly closes what they leave open, and never once says a word about it,
and has been doing it so long that the drunks and dreamers have stopped leaving things open at all.

I have wanted to meet that person my whole life. I'd have hated him on sight.

---

The viewing was Thursday, and the trade goes in the front, which I resented, because I have never in
eleven years walked in the front of anything.

I went as Miss Vale, agent, with a letter from a collector in Wickham Salt who does not exist and a
bill of introduction I had made myself in a back room off Quill Street for two shillings and a promise.
Grey coat, good gloves, spectacles I don't need. Nobody looks at a woman with a notebook. A woman with
a notebook is furniture with an opinion.

The long gallery of the Cage is the most beautiful room in Cinderwick and I would burn it down
tomorrow. Glass cases down both sides, gas turned low so the curios glitter, green baize under
everything, and the whole trade of the Reach drifting up and down it in twos and threes, murmuring,
pricing each other's coats. Nine porters. I counted them twice and got nine both times, and every one
of them stood where he could see two cases and be seen from three, which is a rotation designed by
somebody who has actually robbed a building.

One door in. One door out. Same door.

And lot forty-one, alone, at the top of the room, under its own glass, on its own baize, with its own
lamp.

I want to be professional about this and I can't quite manage it.

It's smaller than they say. It sits about as high as my palm — a brass bird, plain as a sparrow, with
enamel laid into the wings in a blue that has gone a little green with age, and a key in its back the
size of a thumbnail, and one black bead eye. It is not a lovely thing. It is a *made* thing. Somebody
sat at a bench three hundred years ago and cut those feathers one at a time, and I stood in front of the
glass with my useless notebook and my heart going like a picklock and I thought: *there you are, then.*

Forty years in a vault under the Ordinal's business room. Eleven years since a man stood in that
building and said *Junia Wren* out loud to a clerk and took money for it.

Everything it has ever heard, it still has.

The auctioneer's boy came up at four and wound it for the trade, the way they do — a courtesy, a little
theatre, keep the buyers hungry — and the gallery went quiet and the bird opened its brass beak and sang
back a church bell, whole and clean and *there*, so exactly there that half the room looked up for a
steeple that was three streets and forty years away. Then a woman laughing. Then a scrap of a violin,
badly played, and somebody in the room laughed at the badness of it, and the bird stopped, and the
gallery breathed out and gave it a soft little patter of applause the way you'd applaud a clever child.

I didn't watch the bird.

I'd come three days for one thing and this was it: I stood with my back half-turned and I watched the
*room* while the Nightingale sang, and I watched the faces, because a face in front of a curio will tell
you what a man wants, and what a man wants is the whole of my trade.

Three hundred people, delighted.

And at the top of the gallery, in the doorway of a little private office with a green lamp behind him, a
tall grey gentleman who was not delighted at all.

He was listening. Not enjoying — *listening*, with his head very slightly turned, the way you listen at
a door, the way you listen for the one sound in the street that means the thing you've been dreading has
finally come. He listened to the bell and the laughing woman and the bad violin, and he did not move,
and when the bird stopped he closed his eyes for exactly as long as it takes to be grateful.

Then he opened them and looked directly at me, and smiled, and came down the gallery.

---

"Miss Vale," said Magister Crane.

I have met four men in my life who frightened me and three of them were shouting. Crane was about sixty,
neat as a folded letter, grey glove, grey eye, a voice you'd want reading to you when you were ill. He
did not loom. He stopped at a courteous distance and put his hands behind his back like a man admiring a
window-box.

"Magister," I said. "You have me at a disadvantage."

"I don't think anyone has ever had you at a disadvantage." He said it warmly. "You've been three days on
my north wall. Gorse — the porter — enjoyed the gin very much and has told everyone in the yard about the
generous lady, and I'd have you know he's a good man with four children and I have not dismissed him and
I don't intend to. He couldn't have told you anything. There isn't anything to tell." He nodded at my
notebook. "Are you buying, or are you looking?"

"I'm agenting."

"For a Mr Halloran of Wickham Salt." Crane's smile did not move at all. "Who has been dead for six years,
though I grant you his letterhead has aged beautifully. Two shillings, off Quill Street? He does lovely
work. He also drinks, and he'll tell anyone." He let that sit exactly one second — not two, he was too
good for two — and then he tilted his head at the case. "What do you think of her?"

And that, if you want it, is the whole of Magister Crane: he had me by the throat, in his own house,
with nine porters in the room, and what he wanted to talk about was the bird.

"I think," I said, "that she's not for sale."

"No," he agreed, pleased, the way a schoolmaster is pleased. "Lot forty-one will be shown and withdrawn.
It has been shown and withdrawn eleven times in forty years. The Ordinal likes to be seen to have things
it will not part with; it's a kind of advertisement." He looked at the Nightingale, and for a moment his
face did something complicated and private that he put away almost fast enough. "And a house should keep
something back. Don't you find? A house that will sell you anything is only a shop."

"Then why show it at all?"

"Because it draws the right people in." He turned from the glass and looked at me with mild, terrible
kindness. "Now. You're here on Pell's business, and Pell's principal has offered you four hundred
guineas, and you took it, and you took it *knowing*, which is why I have been looking forward to this
conversation for two years."

The gallery went on murmuring. Three hundred people, and I have never been so alone in a room.

I did not say *how do you know about Pell*, because I already had it, and I'd had it since the brass
window in Coldharbour Lane, and I'd been walking around it for nine days the way you walk around a hole
in the floor you've decided isn't there.

The clerk at the consignment window hadn't sent me to a client. He'd handed me a bidder's chit —
*two-fourteen*, printed, waiting, *before I said a word about what I wanted*. There's no fence in
Cinderwick who could reach into the Ordinal's own wall and have a number ready for me.

There's one man who could.

"You wrote the card," I said.

"I wrote the card."

"And Pell's principal—"

"Is a fiction, and a rather expensive one, and Pell is a nervous man who believes he is deceiving me and
sleeps badly about it, and I'd take it as a personal kindness if you didn't disabuse him." Crane drew a
small square of pasteboard from his glove and held it out. Not offered — *held out*, the way you hold out
your hand to a dog you have already fed. "Two-fourteen. Yours. Wednesday night, nine o'clock, the front
steps, between the iron birds, with the rest of the trade."

I looked at it. I did not take it.

"You're inviting me to *bid*."

"I am inviting you to walk in through my front door, in the light, in a good coat, with a number in your
hand and your name on my ledger, and be *seen* doing it." He said it gently. He said all of it gently.
"Miss Vale, you have spent eleven years coming into rooms through the coal. It has made you the finest
finder in this city and it has cost you every single thing you might have had, and you are going to do it
one more time on Wednesday, and I would very much rather you didn't, because you would be so much more
*useful* to me alive."

He put the chit into my hand and closed my fingers over it with one grey glove, and his hand was warm,
and that was the worst part.

"Four hundred guineas and a page from a drawer," said Magister Crane. "That's what Pell's principal
offered, and I'm told you're worth a good deal more than that, and I should know — I've had eleven years
to price you." He stepped back and gave me a small, entirely sincere bow. "Come to the auction, Miss
Vale. Bid on something small. Lose graciously. Then come upstairs and let's talk about what you actually
want, which is not four hundred guineas, and is not, I think, a small house on a good street."

"And if I'd rather come in through the coal?"

"Then I shall be very sorry," he said, "and the doors will all be open anyway."

He went back up the gallery to his office with the green lamp, and the trade parted for him and closed
again like water, and I stood in the most beautiful room in Cinderwick holding a bidder's chit in a hand
I could not entirely feel.

---

I got out. Front door, between the iron birds, in the light, which I have not stopped thinking about.

Here's what I had, walking down Cage Hill with my heart still going: the job was Crane's. The client was
Crane's. The trap was Crane's, and it was not a trap at all, it was a *hire*, and he'd built the whole
thing — the card, the name, the ledger page, nine days of me being clever — for no other reason than to
sit me in a chair upstairs and offer me a wage.

Any sensible finder in the Reach would have taken the boat that night.

But I had one thing he hadn't given me, and I'd taken it off his own face, in his own gallery, while
three hundred people applauded a brass bird.

He was afraid of it.

Magister Crane, who has never raised his voice, who knew about Halloran and Quill Street and the gin and
the coal chute — Magister Crane stood in the doorway of his own office and *listened* to that
Nightingale like a man waiting to hear his own name.

Forty years he's kept it in the vault under the room where the Ordinal does its business. Forty years of
letting it listen. And he has never, in all that time, let it out of the house — and he wound it up in
front of the entire trade on Thursday afternoon and stood there and *braced*.

It's got something of his in it.

So: Wednesday, nine o'clock. The front steps, between the iron birds, with a number in my hand and my
name on his ledger, exactly as instructed, like a good and useful girl.

And somewhere under that floor, in the dark, a small brass creature that knows what he sounds like when
he isn't being polite.
