title: The Office Knows

I filed the survey.

I have turned that decision over more times than I have turned over any other in my life, and I still
cannot tell you whether it was courage or cowardice or simply the habit of thirty-one years. A
cartographer measures and a cartographer files. I had measured. So at the change of watch I went up to
the Office with my stylus-plate under my arm, and I logged Fourteen-aft at one hundred and sixty-nine
point one one metres, and against the entry, in the remarks field where four hundred years of my
predecessors had written *confirmed*, I wrote: *bulkhead at 161 m is not hull. Hatch behind. Deck
uncharted, surveyed, drawing appended.*

Then I went and ate a bowl of grain in the Office refectory with people I have known my whole life,
and listened to Havel from Hydroponics complain about the tomato yield, and said nothing, and my hands
did not shake, and the ordinary running of the world went on all around me exactly as it had the day
before, which I found unbearable in a way I could not have explained to any of them.

I expected — I don't know what I expected. An alarm. A hearing. Men from Ship's Order at my bunk in the
night. What I got, at the end of the sleep-watch, was a chit in my slot, printed on the same recycled
stock as a duty roster, in the same tired grey type that has told me where to go every day since I was
sixteen.

*RENN, S. — 0600, Chart Room 2. Bring the plate. — VOSSE.*

Master Cartographer Ilkka Vosse had taught me to draw. He had taught my mother's mother to draw. He was
the oldest man in the Office by twenty years and possibly the oldest man on the ship, a dry, slow, very
kind person with the stoop of a lifetime spent over a drafting table and hands that still, at his age,
laid down a line you could not improve. In eleven years of working under him I had never once seen him
raise his voice, and I had never once been afraid of him.

I was afraid of him now, and the reason I was afraid of him was simple and it had been sitting in the
back of my skull since the moment my lamp found it.

The chair on Deck Nought was worn smooth. The track across the dust was two feet wide and walked and
re-walked for years. Whoever went down there went down often, and went down alone, and had a key.

---

Chart Room 2 at 0600 is the quietest place on the *Ark Meridian*. The master chart hangs there — the
true one, the physical one, four hundred years of ink on a sheet of laminate three metres by five,
every deck and corridor and coolant gate of the world we live in, drawn and redrawn and initialled by
every hand that ever held the Office's stylus. I have loved that sheet the way other people love a
person. As a child I was allowed to stand in front of it on founding-day and find my own corridor, and
put my finger on the little box that was my family's compartment, and know exactly where in the dark I
was.

Vosse was already there when I came in, with my drawing of Deck Nought spread on the table in front of
him and his spectacles pushed up into his white hair. He had made tea. There were two cups.

"Sit down, Sela," he said. "You draw a beautiful hatch."

I did not sit down. "It's on no chart."

"No."

"It's eight metres of corridor that four hundred years of this Office has recorded as solid hull."

"Yes," said Vosse, and he picked up my drawing and looked at it with what I can only describe as
professional pleasure, tilting it to the light, the way he had looked at my work when I was fourteen.
"You laid the hatch-seam in properly. Most people fudge a seam. It's the hardest line in the trade —
you have to draw the thing you *felt* and not the thing you can see." He set it down. "How long did you
stand at the window?"

The room went a long way off.

I have replayed that half-second more often than any other in this account, and what I remember is not
fear. It is the small, ridiculous, wounded *outrage* of a person who has been the only one awake in the
dark for two days and discovers she was never alone in it at all.

"You've been down there," I said.

"Once a month for forty-one years." He poured the tea. "Sit down, Sela, please. Your legs have gone."

They had. I sat down.

---

He did not defend himself. That is the thing I want recorded, because it would have been easier for me
if he had. He simply told me, in the flat unhurried voice in which he had taught me hatch-seams and
frame-numbering and the correct abbreviation for a coolant gate, and he told me in the right order,
which is a mercy, and which is also, I understand now, a technique.

The founders knew. That was first. They ran the numbers at the window on Deck Nought while the drive
was still burning and they had already seen, in the arithmetic, how it would go — the fuel, the mass,
the ten thousand years, the *if ever*. And they made a decision, in that cold room, about what a map is
for. Then they walled it off, and drew the chart to the wall, and went up the corridor and told their
children the morning was coming.

The Office kept it. That was second. Not a cabal, not a council, nothing so grand: one cartographer at
a time, each of whom found the discrepancy the way I had found it — because the discrepancy is not
hidden, not really; it is eight honest metres in an honest corridor, and it waits for anyone who trusts
their own two feet more than the record. Some found it at twenty. Some found it at sixty. One or two,
Vosse said, never found it at all, and drew their beautiful charts and died happy, and he did not say
this bitterly.

And each of them, having found it, sat where I was sitting, and was told, and then made a choice; and
every one of them, for four hundred years, had chosen to keep drawing the map to the wall.

He got up, then, and went to the long cabinet under the master chart, and opened a drawer I had opened
a thousand times and never once looked at properly — the resurvey drawer, Deck Fourteen, aft-spinward,
the dull archived filings of a corridor nobody cares about — and he took out the sheaf and laid it in
front of me and let me read it myself, which was the cruellest kind thing anyone has ever done to me.

Thirty-one filings. Four hundred years. Every one of them logging Fourteen-aft at one hundred and
sixty-one metres, *confirmed*.

And underneath them, at the bottom of the drawer, in a separate folder with no label, eleven filings
more. Eleven. In eleven different hands, across four centuries, each one logging one hundred and
sixty-nine, and each one initialled, and each one appended with a drawing of a hatch.

The last drawing before mine was dated forty-one years ago and it was Vosse's, and it was better than
mine.

The one before *that* was dated sixty-three years ago.

I knew the hand. I knew it the way you know a face. It was the hand that had drawn the master chart's
whole spinward quarter in a clean confident stroke; it was the hand that had guided mine on the stylus
when I was nine years old and had told me — I remember the exact words, I have built a life on the exact
words — *a corridor is honest, Sela, and so are we, and that is the whole of the trade.*

My grandmother had stood at that window. My grandmother had looked at the stars that do not move, and
read the number that means *if ever*, and come back up the corridor, and drawn the world for another
forty years, and taught me to love the map, and gone into the recyclers at ninety-two with it still
inside her, and never said one word.

I put my hands flat on the table because I did not know what else to do with them.

"She wanted to tell you," Vosse said. "At the end. She asked me whether she might."

"And you said no."

"I said she might do as she liked, and that you were nineteen, and that nineteen is very young to be
handed a thing you can't put down." He sat back down across from me. "She decided you should have your
twenties. I've never been sure she was right. I've never been sure she was wrong, either. That is the
condition, Sela. That is what I'm giving you, and I want you to see it clearly before you decide
anything: not the secret. The *not being sure*, for the rest of your life."

"It's a lie," I said. My voice came out perfectly level, which frightened me more than shaking would
have. "We tell eleven thousand people the map is complete. We stand up on founding-day and we say *the
world is known*. Children learn the drift diagram in schoolrooms — I have *drawn* the drift diagram for
children, Ilkka, I drew it last year, near lights sliding past far ones, *this is how we know we are
going somewhere* — and there is no drift, there has been no drift for ninety-one years, and every one
of us knew."

"Eleven of us knew."

"That's worse."

"Yes," said Vosse. "It is. I've thought so for forty-one years." He turned his cup around on the table
without drinking from it. "May I tell you the part that isn't an excuse? It won't help. But I'd like
one person alive to have heard it."

I did not say yes. I did not say no. He told me anyway.

"A corridor is honest," he said. "That's the first thing we teach, and it's true, and it is not the
whole of the trade, and I have never found a way to teach the rest of it to anyone who hadn't already
stood at that window. A map is not the truth, Sela. A map is a *choice about what to draw*, made by a
person, for a person. You leave the coolant runs off the passenger charts — not to deceive; because a
mother finding her way to the infirmary at third watch does not need the coolant runs, she needs the
infirmary. Every line you put in is a line you decided somebody needed. Every line you leave out is the
same decision." He looked up at the master chart, the whole enormous beautiful lie of it, four hundred
years of loving work. "The founders left off a deck. They were not villains and they were not fools.
They asked the only question our trade has ever really asked, which is: *who is walking this corridor,
and what do they need in order to keep walking it?*"

"They needed the truth."

"Perhaps. Tell me what they'd have done with it." He said it without heat, the way he'd ask why I'd
chosen a projection. "Eleven thousand people, a sealed hull, and ten thousand years to nowhere. Tell
me, and I'll listen, because I have been trying to answer that question alone since before your mother
was born and I have never once got to the end of it."

I did not have an answer. I have some now. I did not have one then, sitting in Chart Room 2 with my
grandmother's handwriting in front of me and my tea going cold.

Vosse stood up. He put my drawing of Deck Nought into the unlabelled folder, on top of his own, and put
the folder back in the drawer, and closed it. Then he took something out of his coat pocket and set it
on the table between us, and sat back down, and waited.

It was a key. An old, plain, manual key, worn bright along one edge by a thumb.

"I'm eighty-six," he said. "I have perhaps two years of clean lines left in me, and then my hands will
go and I will not be able to draw a seam, and I would rather hand this to someone than leave it in a
drawer for the ship to find. I am not ordering you and I could not if I wanted to. Take a month. Walk
your corridors. Go down and sit at the window as many times as you need to — that's what it's for; it's
the only honest room on this ship and I have never once been able to stay in it more than an hour."

"And at the end of the month?"

"At the end of the month," said Ilkka Vosse, "come back and tell me what a corridor is."

I picked up the key. I want to be exact about this, because I have been exact about everything else and
I will not spare myself here at the end.

It was warm. He had been carrying it against his body all morning, waiting for me, the way he had
carried it for forty-one years with nobody to give it to; and the warmth of it went into my palm, and
what I felt — under the horror, under my grandmother's handwriting, under eleven thousand people
sleeping inside a story — what I felt, God help me, was *chosen*.

That is the thing they don't put in the schoolroom diagram. It is not fear that keeps a secret for four
hundred years. It is the honour of being trusted with it.
