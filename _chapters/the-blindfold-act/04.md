title: The Understudy

Every act on a mud lot has an understudy, whether the act knows it or not. That is not sentiment,
it is arithmetic. A show that stops is a show that starves, so nothing on the lot is permitted to be
the only one of itself. The wire man breaks a wrist in Ohio and by the second show in Ohio there is a
girl who has watched him from the ring curb every night for a season, doing three-quarters of his
walk and calling it his out of courtesy. The talker loses his voice to a bad oyster and the
ticket-seller discovers a voice he never advertised. We do not hold auditions for these people. They
audition themselves, in the dark, at the rim of the light, by watching — and the awful manners of the
trade are that you teach your understudy *on purpose*, hand over hand, because a professional would
sooner be replaced well than mourned badly.

I have taught a dozen understudies to a dozen acts. It is the proudest thing we do and the most
foolish, and I never once saw the foolishness until that week, because the foolishness is this: you
build, with your own hands and out of love, the person who can go on without you. You teach her every
brick of your wall. And you tell yourself you are being generous, and you never ask what she will do
with the act when your back is turned, because asking is not with-it, and because the finest
instrument the machine ever grew had trained her own replacement and taught the girl, her first week,
how to lock a door from the outside.

I told you last that I put the lamp out, which is a tell, and that I was taking it. I want to be
plain about what the taking cost, because it did not cost a clue. I had every clue by the third
morning — the spare-hasp turn on the snapped padlock, the taught dark of the kerosene chest, the
bills kept and the coin buried by a hand packing *toward* somewhere. What it cost was four more days
of behaving as though I didn't, and that behaving is the only crime in this whole account that is
mine alone, start to finish, with nobody else's fingers anywhere near it.

Let me show you the shape of the thing I was tempted to let stand, because a temptation you can't
see the shape of isn't a temptation, it's a mood, and I have never been moody a day in my life.

By the fourth afternoon the sheriff had a new theory, and it was a good one, which is to say it was
comfortable for everybody who mattered to him. The coin in Perro's chest had killed his drifter, the
way I told you — a stranger robs and runs, and this money had bedded down for the winter. So he did
what a tired man does when one story breaks: he did not look for a truer story, he looked for *two*
stories, because two small comforts weigh the same as one large truth and cost a great deal less to
carry. Somebody on the lot, he decided, had been light-fingered with the take — a carny pinching from
the box, ugly and ordinary, the kind of thing a show settles quiet and a county doesn't hang a man
for. And the Colonel's death — the locked wagon, the old king down against his own stove, the thing
the town actually wanted a rope for — that he set gently back down where it had been at nine in the
morning of the first day.

On Bruno.

I want you to see how the machine let him. Nobody said *Bruno* this time either. We do not, remember;
saying is not the method. Dell simply began, over the fourth supper, to be sad about outsiders — how
you take a man in for a season, hired muscle, no wall and no road, and you never do know, do you, and
isn't it a shame, and a big man from Toledo with a temper nobody's ever *seen*, which he offered as
proof of the temper. And the sheriff, who needed the death to belong to a stranger so it would not
belong to a family he'd have to keep visiting, felt the size of Bruno reassemble itself around the
locked wagon, and did not check his arithmetic, because a town that is losing a carnival on Friday
will settle for a villain it can watch leave town in irons and never has to see again.

Cleared is not brought back. I told you to remember that, and here is the collection: Bruno stood at
the tent flap through Perro's clearing with his hat in his enormous hands, cleared two hours and not
one step further inside the circle than he'd been against the fender, and from that flap it is a very
short walk to a rope, because a man already standing outside the circle is a man already halfway to
the tree. The circle didn't have to push him. It only had to keep not making room, which is the one
thing this show has always been able to do in its sleep.

So there was the shape of it, laid on the boards face-up, and I could read it from across the lot the
way I read a mark's whole marriage off the way he holds his wife's parasol.

Here is what it would cost me to let it stand: nothing.

I want that word to sit by itself, because I have earned the right to be exact about the price of my
own soul and I found it, that week, marked down to nothing. I did not have to lift a hand. I did not
have to say a word against Bruno or a word for anybody. I had only to keep doing the thing I had done
every day of my life, which is to keep my own gaff — to hold the one reading I had never handed the
sheriff, the spare-hasp turn that is nobody's habit on this lot but mine, and let it stay held. The
padlock on the pay-wagon had been closed from the outside by a hand I had taught, and Bruno's is not
a hand I ever taught, because Bruno is one season and no gaff and not with-it, and I could have proved
that too, if I'd wanted to prove things toward the truth instead of away from it. I said nothing on
the first morning, filed it deep, and told myself I was protecting the show. To go on saying nothing
now, four days later, with a rope drifting toward Toledo — that was not a new sin. It was the same
old silence, kept one week longer. It would cost me nothing because I had already paid for it in
advance, fifteen years ago, when I decided that not-looking was a thing a person could do for a
living and call it seeing.

And the reward was real. This is the part the arithmetic teachers leave out. If I kept the gaff, the
show rolled Friday whole. The sheriff got his stranger, the town got its leaving, Dell got his clean
family, and I got — I got Lark. Alive, unhung, six feet away at the cook-house with her face on
straight, mine to keep teaching until the day she was ready to go north on a road of her own choosing
instead of a road that ended at a county tree. I got the wall. I got the blindfold. I got fifteen
more years of being the eye of a thing that refuses to see, which is, I have told you, the most
comfortable place to hide that God ever built, and now it had a girl in it that I loved.

That was the offer. I want to be honest that I turned it over in my hands like a coin I was
considering spending. I am not going to tell you I was above it. I have never in my life been above
anything; I have only ever been beside it, in the dark, watching it get away with things.

I went to look at the padlock again, which I told myself was diligence and was actually the opposite —
it was a woman going back to check that the exit she was thinking of taking was still there.

They had left the pay-wagon roped off, the padlock hanging where the deputy's bar had sprung it, and
in the grey end of that fourth afternoon I stood at the rope with my blindfold in my pocket and did
the one honest thing available to a coward, which is to read what is in front of her even when she
would rather not. The hasp was closed the way I close a hasp. Not the way the Colonel closed one —
he was a jammer, a man who drove the shackle home with the heel of his hand and trusted iron; you
could always tell a lock the Colonel had set because it looked angry. This one was set soft and sure,
the shackle eased down and the spare turn taken on it, the little tradecraft courtesy I drill into a
new hand so the lock won't sing when you walk it back across a quiet lot — *close it like you're
apologizing to it, child, a lock that's been forced hates you and a lock that's been asked stays
friends.* My own voice. My own hands, one remove away.

I taught that turn to exactly the hands you'd think, over the years — a short roll of them, the ones
I trusted with the box and the crate and the dark. The clerk at the back of me had laid that roll on
the table two nights before and I had said *half the lot*, which was a lie, and I knew it was a lie
while I was telling it, because you do not teach the spare-hasp to a rube or a razorback or a man from
Toledo hired for his arms. You teach it to an understudy. You teach it to somebody you are building to
go on without you. And of the short roll of hands I ever trusted with the friendly turn, only one
belonged to a person with light feet and a bruise she wouldn't name and no road behind her and a
whole life she was trying to buy the fare out of, one dime and one lesson at a time.

I stood at that rope until the light went, reading a padlock I had already read, the way you keep
pressing a sore tooth to be sure it still hurts, hoping this once it won't.

It hurt.

She was at the cook-house when I came back, cutting bread, because that girl cut bread whenever the
world got large — it is a thing you can do with your hands that has an end to it, a loaf you can
finish, and I have watched her reach for a knife and a loaf on every bad night of her year and eleven
days with us, and I never once, until that week, let myself ask what was so large in the world of a
sixteen-year-old that she needed a task with an ending quite so often.

I did the thing I had refused to do for three days. I read her on purpose.

I have told you a tell is a thing you take, not a thing you're given, and that the taking is the
whole ugliness of my trade, and I had been declining to take this one — supplying the innocent
reading myself, *a child grieving, the stove is where they found him*, both readings true, no
receipt. That night I stopped supplying it. I came in for coffee I did not want and I stood where I
could see her hands and I did the cruelest kind thing I know, which is to say a dead man's name near a
person and watch the honest animal chained up inside them tap the truth on whatever it can reach.

"Colonel would've liked this weather for the jump," I said, to nobody, in the flat under my warm.
"He always did like a dry road out."

Her hands did not stop this time. That is the part that finished me. On the first morning her hands
had stopped and her thumb had come up and she'd looked at the stove, because on the first morning she
was a frightened child and the wall I built her was still going up. Four days later the wall was
built. She kept cutting. Even, unhurried, the knife going through the loaf on the beat, her face
pleasant and half a step out of the huddle the way it always is — a flawless piece of work, the
arranged calm laid down smooth over the thing underneath, and I knew it was flawless because I had
laid down that exact calm ten thousand times over the exact same kind of thing, and because the one
place the work slipped was a place nobody alive but me would ever think to look: her breathing did not
change, and a person who has just heard the name of the man she watched die and whose breath does not
change at all is a person *managing* their breath, and the only people who manage their breath are
people who have been taught to, and I taught her that too. *You don't hide the fear on your face,
child, the face is amateur night. You hide it in your chest. Breathe like the boredest woman in the
tent and the face takes care of itself.*

She had built the wall out of my bricks and she stood inside it and did not blink, and it was
beautiful, and it was mine, and it was the proof. The understudy had learned the act. She had learned
it so well that she could perform, at sixteen, in front of the one person on earth equipped to catch
her, a calm I could not fault on any point but the single point where it was too good — and being too
good is a thing that only reads to the teacher, and only as heartbreak.

I want to be clear that I was not now *suspecting* Lark. I want to be so clear about this that you
never let me pretend otherwise. I knew. I had known in a doorway two nights before she was ever a
suspect, when a girl said *if a person* and I put my hand on her chest and pushed her back into the
dark. Everything since — the chest, the sack, the hasp, the breath she managed too well — was not me
finding out. It was me building, plank by expensive plank, a case sturdy enough to stand on while I
did the thing I had already decided to do, which was nothing. A cold reader does not gather evidence
to learn the truth. She gathers it to be *allowed* the truth, to have earned it, so that when she
finally acts on the thing she knew for free she can call herself the one who sees instead of the one
who was told and covered her ears.

So there it all was, by the end of the fourth day. I had the girl. I had the how and the where and
the hand. I had the offer, marked down to nothing, and it was the sweetest offer of my life: keep the
gaff you've kept forever, let the machine do to Bruno permanently what it does to outsiders in its
sleep, and keep the child. Nobody would ever know. That is the thing about being the only person who
can see a thing — you are also the only person who can decide it was never there.

And I stood in the cook-house doorway — a doorway, God's own joke, the same shape of frame she had
stood in offering me all of this for free — and I found the one flaw in the offer, and it was not my
virtue. I have looked for my virtue in that moment for the rest of my life and it is not there. The
flaw was smaller and meaner and truer than virtue. It was that the offer required me to leave a face
unread, and I have never once in fifteen years been able to leave a face unread, and hers least of
all. The comfortable road ran straight through the one country I could not cross. To take the
nothing-priced way out I would have had to be the fraud I have always let people think I am — a woman
who cannot really see, who only guesses warm — and the whole cursed engine of me is that I am not a
fraud, I only wish I were, on nights like that one, with all my heart.

I could keep the gaff on the sheriff. I have kept things from tired men my whole life; it is nothing.
But I could not keep it from *her*, and I could not keep it from *me*, and I understood, standing in
that doorway watching her finish the loaf, that I was going to go to her — not to save her, I had no
plan and no nobility, and I want no credit for a courage I did not yet have. I was going to go to her
for the reason a cold reader goes anywhere, the only reason I have ever gone anywhere: because there
was a face in front of me with the true thing written on it, and it had been offered to me once
already, cleanly, in a doorway, and I had refused it and paid four days to buy back what I could have
had for the asking, and I was done being the woman who reads everyone in the world except the one
person trying to be read.

"Lark," I said.

She looked up with her lovely built face, the calm I gave her sitting on it like a mask I had painted,
and she said, "Sonora?", pleasant, unhurried, breathing like the boredest woman in the tent.

"Come to my wagon," I said. "When you've finished the bread."

The knife went still. Not a flinch — she was too good for a flinch now, I had seen to that. It went
still the way a clock goes still, all at once and on purpose, and then it went on cutting.

"Sure," she said. "It's near done."

I went out across the dark lot to my wagon to wait for the girl I loved to come and tell me the thing
I already knew, and to find out, when she did, whether I was going to be the one who sees or only, at
the very last, the one who saw. Behind me at the tent flap, where he had been standing all week
because it is the only place the circle has ever let him stand, Bruno from Toledo turned his hat over
in his hands and watched me go, and did not know he was watching his own trial walk away into the
dark to make up its mind.
