A tell is not a confession. I want that understood before I show you anybody's, because the whole
miserable engine of this story runs on the difference. A confession is a thing a person gives you. A
tell is a thing you take. The body is an honest little animal chained up inside a liar, and it will
tap out the truth on any surface it can reach — a thumb, a jaw, an eye that goes where the mind is
instead of where the manners are. Nobody can stop it. The best you can do, and I would know, is teach
the animal to tap quieter.

So when I tell you the lot was one great orchestra of tells that fourth morning, understand I am not
saying anybody confessed. I am saying that forty frightened people were chained to forty honest
animals, and I stood in the middle of them with my hunger, taking.

Dell had squared us a search.

He called it something else. Dell has never in his life called a thing what it is if a warmer name was
in stock, and the warm name for this was *cooperation* — the show flinging its trunks open with both
hands, nothing hid and nothing held, every wagon and rig and crate offered up to the county's tired
gaze. You must understand what he was buying. A search the law demands is an accusation; a search you
volunteer is an alibi with forty signatures. He walked ahead of the sheriff from wagon to wagon like a
man showing rooms to a buyer, and his whole pink body said the three sentences it always says — *this
is terrible, we are cooperating, we will be gone by Friday* — and I trailed the pair of them at a
working distance and read the lie under it, which was not a lie of facts. Dell's lies never are. It
was the lie of the frame: *look everywhere, openly, and find nothing, and then the nothing will be
official.* He had already decided the nothing. That is what a patch is for.

The deputy found the coin at half past ten, in the prop wagon, in the kerosene chest.

Understand the geography of that. The fire rig lives in its own iron-lined box because canvas burns
and kerosene wants it to, and nobody opens that chest between towns except Perro, because his living
is in there and so is everything on the lot that can kill a show in four minutes. A hand that goes
into the kerosene chest uninvited is a hand nobody envies. The deputy was a thorough boy with no
imagination, God preserve him, and he hauled the tins out one at a time, and under the last of them,
wrapped in a feed sack and heavy as sin, was the silver. The coin take. Three towns of dimes and
quarters and halves, the whole bottom-weight of the Colonel's box, sleeping in the one crate on the
lot that no honest hand would ever wander into.

The folding money was not with it. I want that plain, because everything that matters next hangs on
it: whoever emptied the green box in the pay-wagon had kept the bills and buried the coin. Paper
travels. Silver testifies. Somebody had sat down in the dark with three towns' worth of money and
sorted it into *take* and *leave* — which is not panic, or not only panic; it is tradecraft, it is
packing for a journey, it is a person turning a robbery into a getaway stake with their hands steady
enough to count.

I knew all that at the rim of the crowd in the time it takes to see a sack open. I filed it. You know
where I file things.

What the sheriff knew was simpler and better suited to his needs: the money had never left the lot.
His drifter-from-outside story, the one with Bruno's shape drawn around it, had a hole in it now the
size of a kerosene chest — because a stranger robs and *runs*, and this money had been tucked in for
the winter. He came off Bruno the way water comes off a roof when the pitch changes. I watched it
happen, the whole slow turn of the man, and I will not pretend part of me didn't sing at it, because
they'd have hung that quiet Toledo giant with a clear conscience. But a sheriff who stops needing an
outside name does not stop needing a name. He turns and looks at the inside, all forty of us, with our
trunks flung open and our cooperation shining, and he asks himself whose crate the silver slept in.

And the machine — my machine, my family, the warm wall I have lived behind for fifteen years — made
its little adjustment. I felt it before I could have said it. Dell's voice did not change and that was
the change; he began saying takeable-back sentences about how *of course the chest is common ground,
practically, anyone might —* and then letting them die, so their opposite hung in the air with his
fingerprints nowhere on it. Ynez told four people she had always felt something cold off the fire rig,
always, and had *said* so. Nobody said Perro. Nobody had said Bruno, either. The circle simply began,
by the pressure of forty polite spines, to leave the fire-eater standing outside it — a man in debt in
three towns, refused money by the dead man to his face, whose own crate had coughed up the silver. By
supper the sheriff would have him against the fender in the sun, and this time the town would get to
keep him, because this time the name would come wrapped in the show's own cooperation.

Here is where I am supposed to tell you I agonized. I didn't. I had done my agonizing in a doorway two
nights before and learned what my agonizing is worth. What I felt watching the wall lean toward Perro
was the cold clerk at the back of me clearing her throat, and what she said was: *he did not do it,
and you can prove it, and it will cost him everything he has except his neck.* And I stood up from the
cook-house bench and went and did it, not because I am brave, but because I have never in my life been
able to leave a face unread, and his was the face they were about to stop reading forever.

I did it in the tent, in front of the sheriff and Dell and whoever's ears the canvas let in, because a
quiet clearing clears nothing — quiet is where the machine lives, and I needed the loud. I sat Perro
down across a table from me like a mark, which he was not, and I am sorry for it to this day, and I
read him out plain with the blindfold in my pocket and my own face bare.

I read the letter over his heart, and made him lay it on the boards. Three towns, three debts, one
kind of man holding all three papers — the kind that writes once. I read the night he had asked the
Colonel, out by the light plant with his hat in his hands, a grown man begging a father because the
bank had stopped answering, and the Colonel saying what fathers say: *a man pays for his own act.* I
read the two months of him eating nothing and sending everything, the boots resoled with belting, the
fire he swallows for a living going up every night out of a body that weighs less each week — all of
it, out loud, in the level carnival voice that carries to the last row. The animal chained up in him
tapped and tapped. He sat there and let me strip him to the springs in front of the law and the patch
and the family, because he understood, the way the desperate understand, that I was the only door out
of that tent.

And when there was nothing left of his mystery but the shame, I spent that too, and bought his neck
with it.

"Now read the silver," I said to the sheriff. "A man three papers deep doesn't rob a box and *bank*
it. Debt has a mouth. Money near Perro doesn't sleep — it goes, same night, first post, because the
kind of man who writes once doesn't write twice. That sack sat under his tins for three days and not a
dime walked. His debts are his alibi. The letter's the proof of them. And whoever kept the paper and
buried the coin was packing *light*, for a journey, toward something — a person with somewhere to get
to. Perro has nowhere to get to. That's what the letter means. You're holding a man whose whole
misfortune is that he *stayed*."

The sheriff looked at the letter a long time. He was not a stupid man, our sheriff; he was a tired one
being handed a better reading than the one he'd bought, by a woman in spangles, in front of witnesses,
with Friday leaning on him. He took it. They do, when it's cold and it's clean. Nobody apologized —
to Perro, I mean; nobody ever apologized to Bruno either, who stood at the tent flap through the whole
performance with his hat in his hands, cleared two hours since and not one step further inside the
circle than he'd been against the fender. Cleared is not brought back. You will want to remember that
in three chapters.

Perro gathered his letter off the boards, and folded it along its old creases, and put it back in the
pocket over his heart, where it must have weighed a different amount now that everyone had read it. He
did not thank me. At the flap he stopped, with his back to me, and said, in the voice he uses when the
torch is out: "You could have asked me quiet."

I could have. Quiet was six feet away and I had a whole minute. I didn't say that. He went out.

That night I sat in my wagon with the lamp low and did the thing you have been waiting a chapter for
me to do, the thing the clerk had been holding out to me since half past ten like a bill on a salver.
I read the hider.

Kept the bills, buried the coin: light feet, a journey, a *toward*. The kerosene chest: not luck. It
is the best dark on the lot, and I know it is, because finding the dark where no honest hand reaches
is a thing I teach — I stood one girl or another in front of half the hiding holes on this show over
the years and said *hot goes next to what burns, child; a searcher's fear is the only lock that never
wears.* And the padlock on the pay-wagon, hanging snapped on its outside staple in the grey of that
third morning, fixed with the spare-hasp turn that nobody's habit on this lot but mine ever taught
anybody. I had not given the sheriff that one. I am telling you in the order I knew things, and I knew
that one first of all and had filed it deepest, and there is a reading of *that*, too, if you want it.

Taught. The hider was taught. Somewhere on that dark lot was a person with a stake in folding money
and a lesson of mine in each hand, packing toward somewhere, and the clerk at the back of me laid the
class roll on the table and it was not long, and I looked at it the way you look at a hand of cards
you already know is bad.

Half the lot, I told myself. Over the years I must have taught half the lot.

Then I put the lamp out, which is a tell, and I am taking it.
