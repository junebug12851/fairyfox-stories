Vera Brandt audits routes the way some people restore paintings: patiently, without mercy, and out
of something close to love. She has spent a career unpicking cooked numbers, and every single one —
this is the point she will make if you give her the opening — was covering a cut corner, and under
the corner, a person. Her creed fits on an index card: *a fudged log is always somebody's cover.*

## What she wants (and needs)

- **Wants:** Route 9's eleven years of impossible minutes explained, filed, and closed — the public
  record made true again.
- **Needs:** to discover that a record can be accurate and still not be true; and to stand under a
  certain lit window she has driven past, by other routes, for nine years.

## The honest opposition

Vera is not a villain and is never written as one. She is right about what records are for, right
that secrets rot institutions, and right that Abel Mensah is lying to her. The book's argument with
her is conducted fairly — and costs her something honest before it's done.

## Voice

Precise, level, faintly amused at her own reputation; asks short questions and writes down the
whole answer, including the pause.
