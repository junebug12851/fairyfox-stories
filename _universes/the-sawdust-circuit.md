The Sawdust Circuit is the loop a travelling carnival cuts across the map — the county fairs and
lot-corners of a dust-and-diner America in the years between the wars, where a show rolls into a
nowhere town on a Tuesday, blazes for three nights of kerosene and calliope, and is gone by Friday
with the town's spare dimes and one or two of its runaways. There is no magic here and never was.
The wonder is all worked: the two-headed calf is a taxidermy, the mind-reader is a cold-reader, the
strongman's dumbbell is hollow on the far side. That's the joke the show is in on and the mark is
not, and the whole realm runs on the sweet electric second before the mark figures it out — or
never does.

A show is a **country of its own**, with its own tongue, its own law, and its own loyalty. Outsiders
are *marks* or *rubes* or *townies*; the people of the show are *with it* — and the first commandment
of the life is *with it and for it*, meaning you protect your own and you never, ever tip the gaff to
a townie. That code is the realm's engine: it makes a family out of thieves and a wall out of a
family, and most every tale on the Circuit is about what happens when the wall has to choose.

## Ground rules

- **Our world, no magic.** 1920s carnival America — ten-in-one tents, a midway, a mud lot, a long
  string of towns. Every "power" is a technique: cold-reading, misdirection, the fixed wheel. Anyone
  claiming otherwise is running a bit.
- **The show is a sealed family.** *With it and for it.* You cover your own, you keep the gaff, you
  take the road over the law every time. Breaking that code is the gravest thing a person here can do.
- **The law is a thing to be squared, not trusted.** Small-town sheriffs and the show's *patch* (the
  fixer who pays the fines and smooths the trouble) circle each other every stop. The badge wants the
  show gone; the show wants three clean nights and the open road.
- **Dark, never lurid.** The Circuit carries real trouble — debt, grift, violence at the edges, a
  death now and then — but the danger is worked like everything else: implied, off-canvas, restrained.
  Menace and mystery, not gore.

## Places

- **The lot / the tober** — whatever muddy field the show sets up on this week; home for three days.
- **The ten-in-one** — the long sideshow tent: ten acts under one banner, one ticket.
- **The midway** — the lit corridor of games, rides, and grab-joints where the money moves.
- **The pay-wagon** — the owner's locked wagon where the week's take sleeps; the show's heart and safe.
- **The back end** — the private strip of trailers and cook-tent where the show lives off-stage.

## Figures & forces

- **The show family** — talkers, readers, strong acts, ride-jocks, cook-house crew: a family bound by
  the code, not blood.
- **The patch** — the fixer who squares the local law with cash and charm; the show's diplomat.
- **The mark / the tip** — the townsfolk and the crowd; the show's prey and its livelihood both.
- **The road / the Circuit** — the itinerary itself, a force: it never stops, and it leaves no one
  time to grieve, confess, or be caught.

## Motifs & texture

Sawdust and trampled grass. Kerosene flare and the smell of it. Canvas snapping, calliope wheeze, the
tinny bark of a talker working a tip. Chalked banners of impossible acts. The particular hush of a lot
after the last townie's gone — a show alone with itself. Everyone wears a shed name.

## Canon & latitude

Fixed: no supernatural anything; the code (*with it and for it*); the show as sealed family; the law as
adversary-to-be-squared; the restrained, non-graphic register. Free: which show, which decade of the
'20s–'30s, which acts, which town, which crime — the Circuit is long and can hold many tales along it.

## Seeds

- A cold-reader who must, for once, read someone she loves — and can't stop seeing it (*The Blindfold
  Act*).
- The patch who squares the wrong sheriff and owes a favour that comes due a hundred miles on.
- A townie who runs off with the show and turns out to be running *from* something the show can't square.
