Posy Fell writes small on purpose. Not slight — *small*, the way a good short story is small: one
launderette, one bus, one hour, one person learning to let themselves be helped. The prose is
warm and quick, with a comic's timing and a soft spot the size of a city; Fell will make you laugh
on one line and go quiet on the next, and never make a fuss about either.

The recurring subject is the tenderness of ordinary places at ordinary hours — and the particular
kind of lonely person who is endlessly kind to strangers and hopeless at being kind to themselves.
Fell believes in small mercies and doesn't trust grand gestures. The magic, when it comes, is
domestic and shy, and the point is never the magic.

*Writes in the Hollow Hours.*
