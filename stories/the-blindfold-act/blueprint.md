**Universe:** the Sawdust Circuit · **Author:** Roan Cassady · **Status:** growing ·
**Marked: complete · no sequel** — a standalone, meant to simply end. The whole case, spoiled on
purpose.

## Why a novelette (as a mystery)

A mystery wants one clean spine: a body, a closed circle, an investigator with a reason to dig, a
turn that recolours everything before it. Six brisk chapters — discovery, the reading of the circle,
the false lead, the turn toward home, the confrontation, the choice. Long enough for a real
investigation and a real moral vise; short enough to keep the wire taut, no wandering B-plot. A single
sitting, propulsive, dark but not bleak.

## Premise

It is 1926, and the Aurelio Bros. carnival is stranded overnight in a nowhere town when **Colonel
Aurelio** — owner, patriarch, landlord of the whole sealed family — is found dead in his **locked
pay-wagon**, the week's take gone. The county sheriff wants it hung on the show and the show gone by
Friday; the show wants it buried and the road taken before the story spreads. **Madame Sonora**, the
carnival's mind-reader — a cold-reader and fraud who has spent fifteen years seeing through strangers
and letting no one see her — starts working her own family for the truth, to keep the law off them.
And she reads it: the one face she never wanted to read. The killer is **Lark**, sixteen, her
apprentice, the girl she took in and taught every trick she'd need to get free.

## The arc (want vs. need)

Sonora **wants** the show unbroken — the family that made a hungry runaway into somebody, the act that
keeps her safe behind a blindfold, the open road on Friday. She **needs** to stop hiding inside Madame
Sonora: to take the act off, be *seen*, and love a person more than the wall she built to protect
herself. The case teaches by cornering her — because the comfortable, monstrous option (let the law
hang an outsider, keep the girl, keep the show, keep the blindfold) sits right there, costing her
nothing, and she has to become someone who won't take it.

## The circle & the truth of that night

The Colonel was no monster and no saint — the show's father and its landlord both, loved and owed and
resented in the same breath. He had found out Lark was skimming the take, dime by dime, building fare
for a run north and a name of her own. On the last night he cornered her in the pay-wagon and threatened
to send her back to the town — and the person — she'd run from. In her terror she shoved him; he went
down against the iron stove and did not get up. She took the box (her escape fund, and panic) and
**locked the wagon behind her with a key she'd copied** — a lock-and-key trick Sonora herself had
taught her. That copied key is the locked room; the relocking is the tell.

## The case (six chapters)

1. **The Pay-Wagon.** First night, nowhere town. The body, the locked wagon, the missing take, the
   sheriff who smells an easy close, the show that wants to roll. Sonora's voice, the act, the family.
   *(written)*
2. **A Cold Reading.** The sheriff fixes on **Bruno** the strongman — an outside roughneck, a perfect
   townie's villain. To keep the law off her people, Sonora starts reading the circle herself:
   introduce the suspects, the code (*with it and for it*), and Lark, her apprentice.
3. **The Tells.** The investigation deepens — a fire-eater's debt, the fortune-teller's grudge, the
   patch's squared-away lies — and the missing money half-surfaces. Sonora clears the easy answers and
   narrows the circle. A false lead that costs.
4. **The Understudy.** The breath, then the turn toward home: Sonora catches the one thing only she
   would — the wagon was relocked with a trick she taught Lark, and Lark's tell when the Colonel's name
   comes up. She knows. And the comfortable way out — let Bruno hang, keep the girl and the show — opens
   in front of her, costing nothing.
5. **What She Taught Her.** Sonora and Lark, alone. The truth of that night comes out — the skimming,
   the threat, the shove, the panic. Sonora has every fact and no comfortable place left to stand. The
   vise closes: the act she loves, the family, the road — against the girl.
6. **The Blindfold Act.** The earned ending. Sonora refuses the monstrous easy thing and takes a third
   road that costs her everything: she clears the innocent, squares the death as the accident it was
   without hanging the girl, and puts Lark on a bus north with the take reframed as a stake — a real
   way out. To do it she burns her own standing, breaks the code, and loses her place in the show and
   the one person she loved, who must vanish clean and whom she will never see again. She takes the
   blindfold off at last, and it costs her the act, the family, and the road. Poorer, seen, and larger.

## Ending

Sonora wins not by out-reading the room but by finally *being* read — by refusing to stay hidden and
paying full price to set the girl free. She loses Madame Sonora and everything the name protected, and
becomes, for the first time in fifteen years, a person. Restrained throughout: the death is off-page
and un-lurid, the horror is moral, not graphic. Dark, propulsive, and humane — and complete in itself.
**Marked complete · no sequel:** this is a story meant to end here.

## Content note

A carnival murder mystery. Contains a death (an accidental one, discovered — never depicted in
graphic detail), grief, betrayal, and a hard moral choice. A frightened teenage runaway is central;
her fear and her past are handled with care and nothing about them is explicit or sensational. No
gore, no NSFW, nothing gratuitous — dark, but reachable and not bleak.
